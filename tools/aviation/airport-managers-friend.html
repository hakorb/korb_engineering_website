Working With Consultants

How to select, manage, and hold accountable your A/E consultants

The A/E Consultant Selection Process (Brooks Act & QBS)

▼

If you're spending federal AIP dollars on design or planning services, you must follow Qualifications-Based Selection (QBS) per the Brooks Act. This means you cannot select your consultant based on price alone. Here's how it works:

  1. Advertise the opportunity. Post an RFQ (Request for Qualifications) publicly. Your state may have specific advertising requirements too.
  2. Receive and evaluate SOQs. Firms submit Statements of Qualifications. Evaluate them on experience, key personnel, past performance, understanding of your project, and local knowledge.
  3. Shortlist and interview. Narrow it down to 3-5 firms. Conduct interviews with your selection committee. Score them independently.
  4. Rank firms. Rank them 1, 2, 3 based on qualifications only -- not price.
  5. Negotiate scope and fee with #1. Only after ranking do you negotiate the scope and fee with your top-ranked firm. If you can't reach agreement, move to #2.
Pro Tip

The most common mistake new managers make is letting a consultant's charisma in the interview override their actual qualifications. Ask for specific references from airports similar to yours in size and complexity. Then actually call those references.

Warning

Never discuss fees before completing the ranking. This is a federal requirement for AIP-funded projects. If your FAA ADO finds out you discussed price before selecting, your grant could be in jeopardy.

What if it's not federally funded? Your state may still require QBS for state-funded projects. Even if they don't, QBS is still best practice. Choosing the cheapest consultant for airport work is almost always a bad idea -- you'll pay for it in change orders, delays, and headaches.

What to Look For in an Engineering Consultant

▼

Not all engineering firms are created equal, especially in aviation. Here's what separates the good from the mediocre:

  • Airport-specific experience. A firm that's great at highways may be terrible at airfields. Look for firms with a dedicated aviation practice group.
  • FAA knowledge. They should know AIP grant processes, FAA Advisory Circulars, and the grant assurances inside and out. If they can't explain the difference between an EA and a CE without looking it up, keep looking.
  • Responsive project manager. The PM is more important than the firm name. A great firm with a distracted PM will give you worse results than a decent firm with an excellent PM.
  • Right-sized for your airport. A massive national firm may treat your GA airport as an afterthought. A small local firm may not have the depth for complex projects. Find the right fit.
  • Strong subconsultant relationships. Geotechnical, survey, environmental -- your prime consultant should have solid subs they regularly work with.
  • Familiarity with your state's aviation program. Each state DOT aviation division has its own quirks. Local knowledge matters.
  • Communication style that matches yours. If you want weekly updates and they're used to "we'll call when something comes up," that's a mismatch.
Pro Tip

During interviews, ask: "Tell me about a project that went sideways and how you handled it." Any firm that claims they've never had a problem is either lying or hasn't done enough work to encounter one. You want to know how they handle adversity.

Red Flags With Consultants

▼

Watch for these warning signs. Any one of them is a conversation; two or more is a pattern:

Scope Creep Without Written Change Orders
Your consultant starts doing work outside the original scope and then bills you for it. Every change in scope should be documented with a written amendment before the work is done. "We went ahead and did it because we thought you'd want it" is not acceptable.

Padding Hours / Billing Irregularities
Review invoices carefully. Are senior partners billing time for tasks a junior engineer should handle? Are travel hours excessive? Is the same meeting showing up on multiple people's timesheets? Request detailed backup with every invoice.

Designing to Their Preferred Contractor
If specs are written so specifically that only one contractor can bid, ask why. Proprietary specifications should be rare and justified. A good consultant designs for competition.

Not Returning Calls or Being Slow to Respond
You're paying them. A 24-48 hour response time for non-urgent matters is reasonable. If you're consistently waiting a week to hear back, that's a problem. Address it directly and early.

Over-Designing (Gold-Plating)
A 10-inch thick apron for single-engine aircraft? A full ILS design study for a 3,000-foot grass strip? Some consultants over-design because their fee is a percentage of construction cost. More expensive construction = higher fee. Always ask: "Is this level of design appropriate for our airport's current and projected use?"

Submitting Boilerplate Specs Not Tailored to Your Airport
If you see another airport's name in the specs (yes, this happens), or if specs reference conditions that don't apply to your airport, your consultant is cutting corners. Your project deserves custom-tailored specifications.

Not Involving You in Design Decisions
A good consultant brings you into the process at key milestones. If they disappear for months and come back with a finished design, that's a problem. You should see the design at 30%, 60%, and 90% completion at minimum.

Pro Tip

Keep a simple log of every interaction with your consultant -- date, who you talked to, what was discussed, any decisions made. This is invaluable if disputes arise later. A quick email summary after phone calls ("Per our conversation today...") creates a paper trail.

How Consultant Fees Work & Typical Ranges

▼

Consultant fees are typically structured in one of three ways:

  • Percentage of Construction Cost: The fee is a percentage of the estimated (or actual) construction cost. Simple to understand, but creates an incentive to increase construction costs.
  • Hourly (Time & Materials): You pay for actual hours worked at agreed-upon rates, usually with a not-to-exceed cap. Good for uncertain scopes.
  • Lump Sum (Fixed Fee): A flat fee for a defined scope. Puts the risk on the consultant to manage their time. Best when the scope is very well-defined.

Typical Fee Ranges by Phase:

PhaseTypical RangeNotes
Planning / Study8-12% of projectMaster plans, environmental, feasibility studies
Design Engineering8-15% of constructionHigher % for complex or smaller projects
Construction Admin (CA)5-8% of constructionFull-time RPR on site drives this higher
Testing / QA2-4% of constructionConcrete, asphalt, soils testing
Note

Smaller projects tend to have higher fee percentages because the fixed costs of mobilization, project management, and document preparation don't scale down proportionally. A $200K project might carry a 15% design fee; a $5M project might be 8-10%.

Pro Tip

Always negotiate the fee structure, not just the number. For projects with uncertain scope, an hourly rate with a not-to-exceed cap protects both parties. For well-defined projects like a straightforward runway overlay, a lump sum keeps things clean.

When to Push Back on Consultant Recommendations

▼

You hired them for their expertise, but you know your airport better than anyone. Push back when:

  • The recommendation doesn't match your airport's actual needs or traffic levels
  • The cost seems disproportionate to the benefit
  • They can't clearly explain why something is necessary
  • The recommendation seems driven by "that's how we always do it" rather than your specific situation
  • They're recommending the most expensive option without presenting alternatives
  • The timeline doesn't account for your operational constraints

Ask them to present at least two alternatives with pros, cons, and costs for any major decision. A good consultant will welcome this because it shows you're engaged.

Pro Tip

The magic phrase: "Help me understand why this is the best approach for our airport." This invites explanation without being confrontational. If they can't give you a clear answer, that tells you something.

How to Hold Consultants Accountable

▼
  • Start with a clear scope of work. Vague scopes lead to vague results. Define deliverables, milestones, and deadlines explicitly.
  • Hold regular progress meetings. Biweekly or monthly, depending on the project pace. Document action items and follow up.
  • Tie payments to milestones. Don't pay 90% of the fee when the design is only 60% done.
  • Document everything in writing. Verbal agreements are worth the paper they're printed on.
  • Address issues early. Don't let small problems fester. A frank, professional conversation now prevents a major dispute later.
  • Use performance evaluations. Some states and the FAA have consultant evaluation forms. Use them. They create a record and motivate good performance.
  • Know your contract's remedies. Understand the termination clauses before you need them.
Pro Tip

At the project kickoff, establish the communication protocol: who calls who, response time expectations, how changes are handled, and who has decision-making authority. Setting expectations upfront prevents 90% of consultant frustrations.

On-Call Agreements vs. Project-Specific Contracts

▼

On-Call (Continuing Services) Agreements: A master contract with one or more firms for as-needed services. Work is assigned via task orders. Great for smaller, recurring needs like design for minor rehab projects, environmental reviews, or responding to unexpected issues.

Project-Specific Contracts: A standalone contract for a single defined project. Better for large, complex projects like a full runway reconstruction or new terminal building where you want to select the best team for that specific job.

When to use which:

  • On-call: Tasks under ~$500K construction, quick-turnaround needs, planning studies
  • Project-specific: Major CIP projects, anything over $1M construction, complex environmental work
Note

Having an on-call agreement doesn't mean you can skip QBS for the initial selection. You still need to go through the full QBS process to select your on-call firm(s). The efficiency comes from not having to repeat the process for every small task order.

Negotiation Tips for Scope and Fee

▼
  • Negotiate scope first, fee second. Get complete agreement on what's included before discussing price. This prevents scope creep disputes later.
  • Get an independent cost estimate. Before negotiating, have a reasonable idea of what the work should cost. Your state DOT aviation division may be able to help.
  • Question every line item. Why do you need a senior partner at $275/hr for field observation? Can a staff engineer handle that?
  • Negotiate multipliers, not just rates. The overhead multiplier is often where the real money is. Industry average is around 2.5-3.0x direct labor. Ask them to justify theirs.
  • Include a not-to-exceed amount. Even for hourly contracts. This protects your budget.
  • Define what's NOT included. Exclusions should be listed explicitly. If it's not in the scope, it's not in the fee.
  • Build in a management reserve. For AIP projects, up to 10% of the consultant fee as contingency for unforeseen scope additions.
Pro Tip

Never negotiate against yourself. If the consultant's first fee proposal is $120K and you think it should be $100K, don't offer $105K as a compromise. Ask them to sharpen their pencil and come back with a revised proposal. Let them make the first move toward your number.

Questions to Ask During Consultant Interviews

▼

Go beyond the standard interview. These questions reveal the real capability of the team:

  1. "Who specifically will be our day-to-day project manager, and what is their current workload?"
  2. "Walk me through how you would approach [your specific project]. What's your first step?"
  3. "Tell me about a project at a similar airport that didn't go as planned. What happened and what did you learn?"
  4. "How do you handle a situation where the contractor is not performing and the airport manager is frustrated?"
  5. "What's your approach to keeping us informed? How often will we hear from you?"
  6. "How do you handle scope changes? Walk me through your change order process."
  7. "What do you see as the biggest challenge with this project?"
  8. "Can you provide three airport manager references we can call this week?"
  9. "What's your current capacity? Are you adequately staffed to take on this project within our timeline?"
  10. "How do you approach value engineering? Give me an example where you saved an airport money without sacrificing quality."
Pro Tip

Pay close attention to who does the talking in the interview. If the firm's senior VP does all the presenting but a junior PM will actually run your project, that's a bait-and-switch. Insist that the people who will do the work are the ones in the interview.

Managing Contractors

From bid day to final completion -- how to manage construction on your airport

Understanding the Bid Process

▼

For AIP-funded construction, you'll typically use Invitation for Bids (IFB) with sealed competitive bidding. This means:

  • Low bid wins (assuming the bidder is responsive and responsible)
  • Bids are publicly opened at a specific date/time
  • The scope must be clearly defined in the plans and specifications
  • You generally must accept the low bid unless there's a documented reason not to (e.g., bidder is not responsible)

RFP (Request for Proposals) is used less often for construction but may apply for services like ARFF staffing, FBO selection, or specialized maintenance contracts where qualifications matter as much as price.

Warning

Splitting projects into smaller pieces to stay under bid thresholds is a federal no-no. If it's one project, bid it as one project. The FAA audits for this.

Key steps in the bid process:

  1. Advertise publicly (check state/local requirements for advertising duration)
  2. Issue plans and specs to plan rooms and interested bidders
  3. Hold a pre-bid conference (highly recommended but usually not mandatory)
  4. Answer questions via written addenda (never give verbal answers that aren't in an addendum)
  5. Receive and publicly open sealed bids
  6. Evaluate bids for responsiveness and responsibility
  7. Award to lowest responsive, responsible bidder
  8. Execute contract and issue Notice to Proceed
Pro Tip

Attend the pre-bid conference and site visit yourself. Listen to the questions contractors ask -- they reveal what parts of the project are complex or unclear. If multiple bidders ask about the same thing, your specs may need clarification via addendum.

How to Read a Construction Schedule

▼

Most airport construction schedules use the Critical Path Method (CPM) shown as a Gantt chart. Here's what to focus on:

  • Critical Path: The longest chain of dependent activities. If any critical path activity is delayed, the whole project is delayed. Usually shown in red.
  • Float/Slack: How many days an activity can slip without delaying the project. Activities with zero float are on the critical path.
  • Milestones: Key dates -- start of paving season, runway closure dates, substantial completion. These are your anchors.
  • Predecessors/Successors: What must finish before the next activity can start. Understanding these linkages tells you if a schedule is realistic.
Pro Tip

Ask the contractor to update the schedule monthly and present it at progress meetings. Compare actual progress to planned progress. When they start falling behind on critical path items, that's when you have the "what's your recovery plan?" conversation -- not two weeks before substantial completion.

Common Contractor Tactics to Watch For

▼

Most contractors are honest professionals. But you should know these games because you will encounter them eventually:

Change Order Games (Bid Low, Make It Up on Changes)
Some contractors intentionally bid low on base items and high on estimated quantity items, then push for changes during construction. They may also "find" unforeseen conditions that require expensive change orders. Mitigation: Have your consultant do thorough geotechnical investigation before bidding, and have realistic quantity estimates.

Substituting Lower-Quality Materials
"Equal or better" substitution requests that are not actually equal. Make your consultant rigorously review every substitution request. Check certifications and mill certs.

Not Following Specs and Hoping Nobody Checks
This is why full-time inspection is critical on airport projects. If you don't have a Resident Project Representative (RPR) on site watching the work, you're trusting the contractor to self-police. Some will. Some won't.

Billing for Work Not Yet Completed
Overbilling on pay applications -- claiming 80% complete on an activity that's really 50% done. Your RPR and consultant need to verify quantities and percent complete before you approve any pay application.

Understaffing the Job
Contractor wins the bid, then assigns a skeleton crew because they're spread across too many projects. Result: your project falls behind schedule. Address this at the first sign of inadequate staffing.

Weather Delay Padding
Every rainy day is a claimed weather delay, even if the work scheduled that day was indoors or unaffected by weather. Require that weather delay requests identify the specific activity impacted and how the weather prevented that specific work.

Pro Tip

The best defense against all of these is good daily documentation. Require your RPR to submit daily construction reports with: weather conditions, contractor's workforce count by trade, equipment on site, work performed, materials delivered, visitors, and any issues. Photos every day. This documentation is worth its weight in gold when disputes arise.

Retainage Explained

▼

Retainage is a percentage of each pay application that you withhold until the project is substantially or finally complete. Typically 5-10% for airport projects.

Purpose: It's your leverage to ensure the contractor finishes the project and completes the punch list. Without retainage, a contractor might move their best crews to the next job and leave yours limping to the finish.

Key points:

  • Retainage percentage should be specified in the contract
  • Some contracts allow reducing retainage to 5% after 50% completion
  • Retainage is typically released at final completion (after punch list, NOT at substantial completion)
  • Some states have laws limiting retainage or requiring it to be held in escrow
  • FAA policy generally supports 5% retainage for AIP-funded projects
Pro Tip

Don't release retainage until you have: completed punch list, final quantities reconciled, all testing reports satisfactory, all O&M manuals delivered, as-built drawings submitted, all DBE documentation finalized, and final certified payrolls received. Use retainage as your leverage for getting these closeout items completed.

Substantial vs. Final Completion

▼

Substantial Completion: The project is sufficiently complete that you can use it for its intended purpose, even though minor items remain. This is when the clock stops on liquidated damages (usually) and the warranty period typically begins.

Final Completion: Every last punch list item is done, all documentation is submitted, and the project is 100% finished. This is when you release retainage and close out the contract.

The gap between these two dates is where projects often stall. The contractor has moved on mentally and physically. Stay on them.

Pro Tip

Set a firm deadline for final completion in the contract (typically 30-60 days after substantial completion). If the contractor doesn't close out by then, start withholding beyond retainage. Nothing motivates a contractor like holding their money.

Punch List Best Practices

▼
  • Walk the project with your consultant and contractor together
  • Document every item with photos and specific location descriptions
  • Assign each item a priority (safety items first)
  • Set a deadline for completion of all items
  • Don't accept "we'll fix it under warranty" -- punch list items must be fixed before final acceptance
  • Re-inspect after the contractor claims items are complete
  • Don't let the list grow -- new deficiencies found after substantial completion may be warranty items, not punch list

Liquidated Damages -- When and How to Enforce

▼

Liquidated damages (LDs) are a pre-agreed daily amount the contractor owes if they miss the completion deadline. They're not a penalty -- they represent your actual estimated damages from the delay (lost revenue, extended consultant costs, operational impacts).

  • The LD amount must be reasonable. Courts may not enforce an LD that's clearly punitive rather than a genuine pre-estimate of damages.
  • Document legitimate time extensions. If you granted weather days or change order time, adjust the completion date accordingly.
  • Notify the contractor in writing when LDs begin accruing. Don't spring it on them at the end.
  • Deduct LDs from the final pay application or retainage. Don't try to invoice the contractor separately.
  • Be consistent. If you waive LDs for one contractor but enforce them on another, you may have a legal problem.
Warning

Always consult with your attorney before assessing LDs. The contractor will likely dispute them, and you need to make sure your documentation supports every day you're claiming.

DBE Requirements and Monitoring

▼

AIP-funded projects have Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE) participation goals. Key points:

  • DBE goals are set as a percentage of the contract amount
  • The contractor must either meet the goal or demonstrate good faith efforts to do so
  • Only firms certified by the state's Unified Certification Program (UCP) count
  • Monitor actual DBE utilization throughout construction, not just at bid time
  • Require DBE payment documentation with each pay application
  • The contractor cannot terminate a DBE sub without your consent
  • Watch for "pass-through" arrangements where the DBE is just a front
Pro Tip

Call your DBE subcontractors directly periodically. Ask if they're getting paid on time and if they're performing a commercially useful function (meaning they're actually doing meaningful work, not just passing it through to a non-DBE firm). This is your due diligence.

Prevailing Wage / Davis-Bacon Compliance

▼

AIP-funded projects require paying workers at least the prevailing wage rate as determined by the Department of Labor. Key requirements:

  • Post the wage determination at the job site where workers can see it
  • Certified payrolls must be submitted weekly by the contractor and all subcontractors
  • Review certified payrolls for correct classifications, rates, and overtime
  • Conduct worker interviews periodically to verify actual wages match certified payrolls
  • Common violations: misclassifying workers (calling a carpenter a laborer to pay a lower rate), not paying overtime for hours over 40/week, not paying fringe benefits
Warning

Davis-Bacon violations can result in the contractor being debarred from federal projects. As the airport sponsor, you have a responsibility to monitor compliance. The DOL and FAA both audit for this.

How to Document Everything

▼

In airport construction, if it isn't documented, it didn't happen. Essential documentation:

  • Daily Construction Reports: Weather, crew count, equipment, work performed, materials, visitors, issues. Every day. No exceptions.
  • Photos: Take hundreds. Date and caption them. Before, during, and after for every major activity. You cannot have too many photos.
  • RFIs (Requests for Information): Track every RFI with dates submitted and responded. Slow RFI responses cause delays -- document turnaround times.
  • Meeting Minutes: Distribute within 48 hours. Include action items with responsible parties and deadlines.
  • Testing Reports: All density tests, concrete breaks, asphalt cores, etc. Keep organized by location and date.
  • Correspondence Log: Track every letter, email, and formal communication with dates and subject.
  • Change Order Documentation: Written request, cost proposal, negotiation notes, approval, and directive. For every single change.
Pro Tip

At the end of each construction day, ask yourself: "If this project ended up in a claim or lawsuit, would my documentation from today tell the full story?" If the answer is no, you're not documenting enough.

FAA & Federal Compliance

Grant assurances, Part 139, and everything federal that keeps you up at night

AIP Grant Assurances -- The Big Picture

▼

When you accept AIP grant money, you agree to 39 grant assurances that bind your airport for 20 years (useful life of the funded improvement). These are not suggestions -- they're obligations. Here are the ones that most commonly cause problems:

#5 -- Preserving Rights and Powers
You cannot enter into any agreement that gives away your authority to operate the airport in compliance with the grant assurances. If a lease gives a tenant control over airport operations, you've got a problem.

#22 -- Economic Nondiscrimination
You must make the airport available on reasonable terms and without unjust discrimination. You can't play favorites with tenants or operators. If one FBO gets a sweetheart deal, you may be violating this assurance.

#23 -- Exclusive Rights
You cannot grant an exclusive right for any aeronautical activity. This doesn't mean you must let everyone operate -- you can have reasonable minimum standards. But you can't give one operator a monopoly.

#24 -- Fee and Rental Structure
Your fees and rents must be fair, reasonable, and as close to a self-sustaining rate as possible. You can't charge $1/year rent to your buddy and $10/sq ft to everyone else.

#25 -- Airport Revenue
ALL airport revenue must be used for airport purposes. Period. Revenue diversion is one of the FAA's top enforcement priorities. Using airport funds to subsidize the city's general fund? That's revenue diversion.

#29 -- Airport Layout Plan
You must keep your ALP current and get FAA approval before making any changes to the airport that affect the ALP. Building a new hangar? Check the ALP first.

Critical Warning

Grant assurance obligations can last 20 years or more from the date of the last grant. Even if you stop accepting grants, the clock doesn't reset. And some assurances (like revenue diversion) essentially last forever if you're in the NPIAS. Consult aviation counsel if you're unsure about any compliance issue.

Pro Tip

Download the FAA's "Airport Sponsor Assurances" document and keep it on your desk. When anyone -- city council, tenants, or consultants -- proposes something that doesn't feel right, check it against the grant assurances. They're your shield against bad decisions.

Part 139 Certification Basics

▼

14 CFR Part 139 applies to airports serving scheduled air carrier operations with aircraft having more than 9 passenger seats. If that's your airport, you need a Part 139 Airport Operating Certificate.

Key elements of Part 139:

  • Airport Certification Manual (ACM): Your bible. It describes how you comply with every Part 139 requirement. FAA inspectors check you against your own manual.
  • Self-inspection program: Daily, if you're doing it right. Document everything.
  • ARFF: Aircraft Rescue and Fire Fighting. Staffing, equipment, training, and response time requirements based on your airport's ARFF Index.
  • NOTAM management: Timely, accurate NOTAMs for any condition affecting safety.
  • Wildlife hazard management: Required if you've had a wildlife strike or have attractants near the airport.
  • Airport Emergency Plan (AEP): Reviewed and exercised on a schedule.
Inspection Triggers

FAA inspectors can show up at any time, but they typically do annual certification inspections. They can also visit in response to complaints, incidents, or when you've proposed changes to your ACM. Be ready at all times -- not just before the scheduled inspection.

NOTAM Procedures

▼

NOTAMs (Notices to Air Missions) are how you communicate airport conditions to pilots. Getting NOTAMs right is critical for safety.

  • When to issue: Runway/taxiway closures, lighting outages, construction activity, changed thresholds, unresolved snow/ice, wildlife activity, any safety-affecting condition
  • Timeliness: Issue the NOTAM before the condition exists, not after. Plan ahead for construction NOTAMs.
  • Accuracy: Wrong information is worse than no information. Double-check runway numbers, coordinates, and times.
  • Cancel promptly: When the condition is resolved, cancel the NOTAM immediately. Stale NOTAMs erode pilot trust.
  • Who can issue: Only authorized personnel. Make sure your NOTAM contact list with FSS is current.
Pro Tip

Check your own NOTAMs daily at notams.faa.gov. You'd be surprised how often NOTAMs have errors or stale information. If you wouldn't want to fly into your own airport based on the NOTAMs, fix them.

Obstruction Evaluation (FAR Part 77)

▼

Part 77 defines the imaginary surfaces around airports that protect airspace. Any proposed construction or alteration that might penetrate these surfaces requires an FAA Form 7460-1 ("Notice of Proposed Construction or Alteration").

  • Primary Surface: Extends 200' beyond each end of the runway at the runway elevation
  • Approach Surface: Slopes upward from the end of the primary surface (slope varies by runway type)
  • Transitional Surface: 7:1 slope from the edges of the primary and approach surfaces
  • Horizontal Surface: 150' above the airport elevation, extending outward
  • Conical Surface: 20:1 slope from the horizontal surface outward

When someone wants to build a cell tower, grain elevator, or wind turbine near your airport, have them file a 7460 and let the FAA evaluate it. Don't take "it's fine" as an answer without the FAA's determination.

Pro Tip

Develop a relationship with your local planning and zoning departments. Ask them to notify you of any proposed construction within several miles of the airport. Ideally, get adopted airport overlay zoning that requires a 7460 filing for any construction above certain heights within defined areas. Prevention is far easier than removal.

Wildlife Hazard Management

▼

Wildlife strikes cost the aviation industry billions annually. Your responsibilities:

  • Report all wildlife strikes using FAA Form 5200-7 (even if no damage)
  • Wildlife Hazard Assessment (WHA): Required if you've had a strike causing damage, a multiple strike, or a strike ingested into an engine. USDA Wildlife Services typically conducts these.
  • Wildlife Hazard Management Plan (WHMP): If the WHA determines a hazard exists, you'll need a WHMP approved by the FAA.
  • Eliminate attractants: Standing water, certain vegetation, open dumpsters, agriculture leases near runways -- all can attract wildlife.
  • AC 150/5200-33 provides guidance on hazardous wildlife attractants on or near airports.
Pro Tip

Build a relationship with your local USDA Wildlife Services office. They're the experts and can help you develop cost-effective wildlife management strategies. Many airports have standing agreements with USDA for regular wildlife management visits.

Safety Management System (SMS) Basics

▼

SMS is a proactive approach to safety management. Instead of just reacting to incidents, you systematically identify hazards, assess risks, and implement mitigations before something bad happens.

Four pillars of SMS:

  1. Safety Policy: Management commitment, safety objectives, accountability
  2. Safety Risk Management: Hazard identification, risk assessment, mitigation
  3. Safety Assurance: Monitoring performance, auditing, continuous improvement
  4. Safety Promotion: Training, communication, safety culture

While SMS is currently required for Part 139 airports, the principles are valuable for any airport. Start with simple things: regular safety self-inspections, incident reporting without blame, and a willingness to fix hazards before they cause an accident.

How to Work With Your FAA ADO

▼

Your Airports District Office (ADO) is your primary point of contact with the FAA for airport development and compliance. Build a good relationship:

  • Know your program manager. They're assigned to your airport. Introduce yourself and communicate regularly.
  • No surprises. If you have a problem, tell your ADO early. They'd rather help you fix an issue than discover it during an audit.
  • Submit complete applications. Incomplete grant applications are the #1 cause of delays. Ask your ADO what they need before submitting.
  • Attend their workshops. FAA regional workshops are free and valuable. Network with other airport managers too.
  • Respect their workload. Your ADO manager handles multiple airports. Be organized and prepared when you meet with them.
  • Document your conversations. Follow up phone calls with an email summary.
Pro Tip

Invite your FAA program manager to visit your airport at least once a year, not just during inspections. Walk the airfield together. Show them your improvements. A program manager who knows your airport and your commitment to it is more likely to advocate for your grant applications.

NPIAS, BIL Funding, and State Block Grants

▼

NPIAS (National Plan of Integrated Airport Systems): If your airport is in the NPIAS, you're eligible for AIP funding. The NPIAS identifies approximately 3,300 airports significant to the national air transportation system.

BIL (Bipartisan Infrastructure Law): Provided approximately $25 billion for airport infrastructure through 2026. This includes:

  • Airport Terminal Program (ATP) grants for terminal development
  • Airport Infrastructure Grants (AIG) for traditional AIP-eligible projects
  • Additional funding for air traffic control towers, environmental programs, and more

State Block Grant Program: In participating states, the state aviation agency administers AIP grants for GA airports instead of the FAA. This can mean faster processing and more state-level support. Check if your state participates.

Pro Tip

Keep your CIP (Capital Improvement Plan) current and submitted to both your state aviation agency and FAA ADO annually. Projects not in the CIP rarely get funded. The CIP is your 5-year wish list -- plan ahead because the funding cycle is long.

Types of Airport Agreements

▼

Know your agreement types:

  • FBO Agreement: Your most complex. Covers fuel sales, aircraft services, hangar space, sometimes flight training. Usually includes minimum investment requirements and performance standards.
  • Hangar Lease (Airport-Owned): Airport owns the building, leases individual units. You maintain the structure; tenant maintains interior.
  • Ground Lease: Tenant leases land and builds their own improvements. Longer terms (20-40 years) to allow the tenant to amortize their investment. Improvements typically revert to the airport at lease end.
  • Tie-Down Permit: Short-term agreement for outdoor aircraft parking. Usually month-to-month or annual.
  • Through-the-Fence (TTF): Access from adjacent private property onto the airport. This is a minefield -- proceed with extreme caution (see separate section).
  • Concession Agreement: For restaurants, car rental, parking operators at commercial airports.
  • Agricultural Lease: For farming airport land not needed for aviation use. Keep crops low and wildlife-unfriendly.

Minimum Standards for Commercial Operators

▼

Minimum standards establish the baseline requirements for anyone wanting to provide commercial aeronautical services on your airport. They should address:

  • Required facilities and improvements (hangar size, office space, paved ramp)
  • Hours of operation
  • Staffing requirements (qualified personnel)
  • Insurance minimums
  • Equipment requirements
  • Environmental compliance
  • Customer service standards

Minimum standards must be reasonable and applied equally to all operators in the same category. They can't be set so high that only one operator can meet them (that would effectively grant an exclusive right).

Pro Tip

Review and update your minimum standards every 3-5 years. What was reasonable in 2010 may not reflect today's industry. Get input from current tenants and look at what peer airports require. FAA AC 150/5190-7 is your guide.

How to Set Fair Rental Rates

▼

Setting rental rates is both art and science. Your rates must be:

  • Fair and reasonable (Grant Assurance #24)
  • Nondiscriminatory (similar tenants pay similar rates)
  • Working toward self-sustainability

Methods for establishing rates:

  1. Market Rate Survey: Compare to similar airports in your region. Adjust for local cost of living and airport amenities.
  2. Cost-Based Approach: Calculate the cost of providing the facility (land value, building cost, maintenance, utilities) and set the rate to cover those costs plus a reasonable return.
  3. Appraisal: For ground leases, a certified appraisal of fair market land rental value is the gold standard.
Key Point

Document your rate-setting methodology. If a tenant challenges your rates to the FAA, you need to show you used a reasonable, consistent process. "We've always charged this" is not a methodology.

When to conduct a market rate survey: Every 3-5 years, at lease renewal, when adding new lease categories, or when you suspect your rates are significantly above or below market.

Common Tenant Disputes and How to Handle Them

▼

Subletting Without Permission: Your lease should require written consent for any sublease. If you discover unauthorized subletting, send a written notice of violation and require they either get approval or terminate the sublease. The sublessee needs their own insurance and must meet your standards.

Not Maintaining Their Premises: Include clear maintenance obligations in the lease. Conduct annual inspections and document deficiencies in writing. Give a reasonable cure period, then escalate to default notices.

Unauthorized Activities: Running an auto repair shop out of an aircraft hangar? It happens. Address immediately -- it's a lease violation and potentially an insurance/safety issue. Document and send a cure-or-quit notice.

Insurance Lapses: Require tenants to name the airport as additionally insured and require certificates annually. Set up a tracking system. A tenant without insurance is a massive liability exposure.

Falling Behind on Rent: Address immediately. The longer you wait, the harder it gets. Your lease should have late fees and clear default/termination procedures. Be consistent -- if you let one tenant slide, others will expect the same treatment.

Pro Tip

Handle every dispute in writing, even if you also discuss it verbally. A friendly phone call followed by a letter summarizing the conversation and the agreed-upon resolution creates a paper trail that protects both parties. And always consult your attorney before sending a default or termination notice.

Through-the-Fence Access -- Risks and FAA Stance

▼

Through-the-fence (TTF) access allows property owners adjacent to the airport to access the airfield from their private land. This is one of the most contentious topics in airport management.

The FAA's stance (FAA Order 5190.6B, Section 9.10):

  • The FAA discourages new TTF access agreements, especially residential TTF
  • Residential TTF operators must pay their fair share and comply with airport standards
  • Existing TTF agreements should be brought into compliance at renewal
  • TTF users must sign an access agreement and pay fees comparable to on-airport tenants

Risks of TTF:

  • Loss of airport revenue (they use your runways without paying rent for airport land)
  • Loss of control over who accesses the airfield
  • Security concerns
  • Encroachment of incompatible development near the airport
  • Difficulty enforcing standards on off-airport property
  • Political pressure from adjacent property owners
Warning

If you're being pressured to grant new TTF access, consult with both your aviation attorney and your FAA ADO before agreeing to anything. Once you grant TTF access, it's very difficult to take it back. The FAA has withheld AIP funding from airports with non-compliant TTF operations.

Fuel Flowage Fees Explained

▼

A fuel flowage fee is a per-gallon charge the airport collects from fuel sellers for fuel delivered and sold on the airport. It's one of the most common and reliable airport revenue sources.

  • Typical range: $0.05 to $0.15 per gallon for Avgas; similar or slightly higher for Jet-A
  • How it's collected: FBO reports gallons sold monthly; airport invoices based on reported gallons
  • Self-fueling: If you allow self-fueling (and grant assurances generally say you must allow it), you can charge a flowage fee on self-fueled gallons too
  • Verification: Periodically verify reported gallons against fuel delivery records and tank stick readings
Pro Tip

Include an annual CPI escalation clause in your fuel flowage fee. A nickel per gallon today won't be worth a nickel in ten years. Also, compare your rate to peer airports annually -- don't leave money on the table.

How to Handle FBO Complaints From Pilots

▼

Pilots who visit your airport and have a bad FBO experience may blame the airport. How to handle it:

  1. Listen and document. Take the complaint seriously. Record the details.
  2. Share with the FBO. Give them a chance to address it. Most professional FBOs want to know about service issues.
  3. Look for patterns. One complaint is an incident. Multiple complaints about the same issue are a trend that needs action.
  4. Reference your minimum standards. If the complaint relates to a standard the FBO is required to meet, hold them to it.
  5. Monitor and follow up. Let the pilot know you've addressed it.
Pro Tip

Check your airport's reviews on AirNav.com, ForeFlight, and social media periodically. Pilots talk, and online reviews about rude line staff or outrageous fuel prices can impact your airport's reputation and traffic.

Lease Renewal vs. Rebid

▼

When renewal makes sense:

  • Tenant is meeting or exceeding all lease obligations
  • The lease has a renewal option and tenant has properly exercised it
  • Market conditions haven't changed dramatically
  • The tenant has made significant investment in the premises

When competitive rebid makes sense:

  • Lease has no renewal option
  • Performance has been marginal
  • Market conditions have changed significantly and you want to attract new operators
  • You want to compare the incumbent to potential new operators
  • Grant assurance compliance requires demonstrating nondiscrimination
Key Point

Start the renewal/rebid conversation at least 18-24 months before expiration. This gives time for negotiation, competitive processes, and transition if needed. Rushing leads to bad agreements.

Exclusive Rights -- What's Allowed and What's Not

▼

Grant Assurance #23 prohibits granting exclusive rights for aeronautical activity. But there are nuances:

  • What IS an exclusive right: "Only XYZ Aviation may sell fuel on this airport" or any agreement that effectively prevents competition
  • What is NOT an exclusive right: Reasonable minimum standards that any qualified operator could meet. If only one operator happens to meet your standards, that's not exclusive -- it's a natural monopoly due to market conditions.
  • Airport self-service is allowed: The airport itself can provide any aeronautical service as the operator, even if it prevents private competition in that service
  • Reasonable limitations: You can limit the number of operators if physical space constraints dictate, but you need to document this
Warning

Be very careful with lease language. Even a well-intentioned provision like "tenant shall have first right of refusal for any new hangar space" could be construed as granting an exclusive right. Have aviation counsel review every lease.

How to Evict a Problem Tenant

▼

Eviction is a last resort but sometimes necessary. The general process:

  1. Document the violations. Build your case with dates, photos, written communications, and specific lease provisions violated.
  2. Send a written Notice of Default. Cite specific lease provisions violated. Give the cure period specified in the lease (typically 30 days for non-monetary, 10-15 days for monetary defaults).
  3. Follow up in writing during the cure period. Document whether they're making progress.
  4. If uncured, send a Notice of Termination. Again, following the lease terms exactly.
  5. If they don't vacate, initiate legal proceedings. This is where you absolutely need your attorney.
Critical Warning

Never attempt self-help eviction (locking them out, removing their property, shutting off utilities). Always follow the lease terms exactly and use the legal process. Aviation tenants have aircraft -- a wrongful eviction could result in significant liability. Involve your attorney from step one.

Noise Complaints & Community Relations

Navigating the delicate balance between aviation operations and community expectations

Understanding Noise Contours and DNL

▼

DNL (Day-Night Average Sound Level) is the FAA's standard metric for measuring airport noise exposure. It represents the average noise level over 24 hours, with a 10 dB penalty added for nighttime operations (10 PM - 7 AM).

  • 65 DNL is the FAA's threshold for "significant" noise impact. Residential land use is considered incompatible at or above 65 DNL.
  • Noise contour maps show lines of equal noise exposure around the airport, like topographic elevation lines.
  • Inside 65 DNL: Residential is incompatible. Eligible for noise mitigation (sound insulation, land acquisition).
  • Inside 75 DNL: Even more restrictive. Most land uses except aviation and industrial are incompatible.

Important context: A GA airport with light piston traffic rarely generates noise contours that extend beyond the airport property. This is mainly an issue for commercial service airports and airports with frequent jet or military operations.

Pro Tip

When community members say "it's never been this loud," they may be right -- or their tolerance may have changed because new houses were built closer to the airport. Know your noise contour history. If it hasn't changed but the complaints have increased, that's a land use problem, not an airport problem.

How to Respond to Noise Complaints

▼

Every noise complaint deserves a response. Even if you can't fix the noise, you can demonstrate respect:

  1. Acknowledge promptly. Respond within 48 hours. The complaint may be about noise, but the person is really saying "I feel unheard."
  2. Document the complaint. Date, time, location, nature of the noise, aircraft type if known.
  3. Investigate. Was there unusual activity? A training pattern? Low-flying aircraft? Get the facts.
  4. Respond honestly. If the operations were normal and legal, say so politely. If something was off, acknowledge it.
  5. Explain (but don't excuse). Help them understand what's happening and why, without being dismissive.
  6. Offer what you can. Voluntary noise abatement procedures, preferred runways, restricted hours for training. But never promise what you can't deliver.
Template Response -- General Noise Complaint
Dear [Name], Thank you for contacting us about aircraft noise at your property on [date]. We take all noise concerns seriously and appreciate you bringing this to our attention. After reviewing our records for the date and time you described, [explain what was occurring -- e.g., "operations were consistent with normal traffic patterns" OR "there was training activity that resulted in more traffic than usual on that runway"]. As a public-use airport, we do not have the authority to restrict where or when aircraft fly -- that authority rests with the FAA, which controls the airspace. However, we do have voluntary noise abatement procedures that encourage pilots to [describe relevant procedures]. I have logged your complaint in our noise tracking system. This data helps us understand noise patterns and make informed decisions about noise mitigation when opportunities arise. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this further. Please feel free to contact me at [phone/email]. Respectfully, [Your name, title]

Voluntary Noise Abatement Procedures

▼

Key word: voluntary. As an airport operator, you generally cannot restrict aircraft operations (that's FAA authority under 14 CFR Part 161). But you can establish voluntary procedures that most pilots will follow:

  • Preferential runway use: "When winds permit, Runway [X] is preferred for noise abatement"
  • Voluntary quiet hours: "Pilots are requested to avoid repetitive training operations between 10 PM and 7 AM"
  • Modified traffic patterns: Offset patterns to avoid noise-sensitive areas where operationally safe
  • Altitude recommendations: "Maintain [X] feet AGL until past [landmark]"
  • Run-up area locations: Place run-up pads as far from residential areas as practical

Publish these in your Airport/Facility Directory entry, post them at the FBO, and include them in pilot handouts. Brief local flight schools specifically.

Warning

Do not attempt to impose mandatory noise restrictions without going through the FAR Part 161 process. It's extremely expensive, rarely successful, and attempting to enforce unauthorized restrictions can put your AIP funding at risk. Stick to voluntary measures.

Part 150 Noise Compatibility Studies

▼

14 CFR Part 150 provides a voluntary process for airports to study noise impacts and develop compatible land use programs.

Two components:

  1. Noise Exposure Maps (NEMs): Show current and future (5-year) noise contours. Provide legal protections for the airport against future noise lawsuits within the mapped areas.
  2. Noise Compatibility Program (NCP): A set of recommended measures to reduce noise impact. Can include operational measures, land use measures, and program measures.

Benefits: FAA acceptance of the NCP opens the door to AIP funding for noise mitigation (sound insulation, land acquisition, noise monitoring systems). It also provides legal protections.

Pro Tip

A Part 150 study costs $500K-$2M+ and takes 2-3 years. It's worth it for airports with significant noise issues, especially if you need federal funding for mitigation. But it's not necessary for most GA airports. Talk to your FAA ADO about whether it makes sense for your situation.

Community Outreach Best Practices

▼

The best time to build community goodwill is before you need it. Proactive outreach strategies:

Airport Open Houses / Fly-In Events: Nothing changes perceptions like inviting people onto the airport. Let them sit in aircraft cockpits, meet pilots, see the operation up close. Partner with local EAA chapters and flying clubs.

Airport Advisory Committee: A standing committee of community members, elected officials, pilots, and tenants. Meets quarterly to discuss airport issues. Gives neighbors a voice and gives you a sounding board.

Regular Communication: Quarterly newsletter, active website, social media presence. Share good news: economic impact, jobs supported, community events, capital improvements.

School Tours: Partner with local schools for STEM field trips. Today's curious 5th grader could be tomorrow's A&P mechanic or airline pilot.

Economic Impact Data: Commission an economic impact study and share the results widely. When people understand the airport generates $X million in economic activity and supports Y jobs, the calculus changes.

Pro Tip

Your most vocal critics often just want to be heard. Invite them to serve on your advisory committee. Some will decline, but those who accept often become your strongest advocates once they understand the full picture. It's much harder to demonize something you're part of.

Dealing With Hostile Community Members & NIMBY

▼

Some community members will never be satisfied. How to handle them professionally:

  • Stay calm and factual. Never match their emotion. You represent the airport.
  • Acknowledge their frustration without agreeing with their conclusions. "I understand this is frustrating for you" is different from "You're right, we should close the airport."
  • Don't argue at public meetings. State the facts, offer to meet one-on-one, and move on. Public arguments rarely have winners.
  • Document threats or abusive behavior. If someone threatens you or airport staff, that's a law enforcement matter.
  • Build allies. For every vocal critic, there are usually ten quiet supporters. Activate them.
  • Know your legal standing. The airport was (usually) there first. Grant assurances obligate you to operate it. You can't close the airport because neighbors are unhappy.
Pro Tip

When presenting at city council or public meetings about airport issues, bring your economic impact data, your safety record, and your community outreach track record. Lead with what the airport gives to the community, then address concerns. Data beats emotion in the long run.

Compatible Land Use & Encroachment

▼

Incompatible development around airports is an existential threat. Once houses are built in flight paths, the political pressure to restrict operations becomes enormous.

  • Airport overlay zoning: Work with local government to adopt zoning that restricts noise-sensitive development near the airport. FAA AC 150/5190-4A provides guidance.
  • Avigation easements: For properties near the airport, these legal instruments acknowledge the right of aircraft to overfly and generate noise.
  • Land acquisition: The most effective (and expensive) solution. Buy the land in critical areas and keep it as open space or compatible use.
  • Developer notification: Require real estate disclosures for properties within noise-affected areas.
  • Engage early in planning processes. Monitor local development proposals and comment when they affect the airport.
Warning

Grant Assurance #21 requires airports to take appropriate action to restrict incompatible land use. If you're not actively working with your local government on compatible land use, you may be in violation. Document your efforts even if they're not always successful.

Noise Complaint Tracking

▼

Track every noise complaint, even if you can't do anything about it. The data is invaluable for:

  • Identifying patterns (same time, same flight path, same complainant)
  • Justifying noise abatement measures
  • Supporting Part 150 study applications
  • Defending against claims that you're ignoring the community

Data to record for each complaint:

Pro Tip

Produce a quarterly noise complaint summary for your board or advisory committee. Show trends over time. This demonstrates you're taking the issue seriously and proactively managing it. It also helps identify whether a few serial complainers are skewing the numbers.

Financial Management

Revenue, budgets, rates, and keeping the airport financially healthy

Airport Revenue Sources

▼

Aeronautical Revenue (from aviation activities):

  • Fuel flowage fees: Per-gallon charge on fuel sold. Often the largest single revenue source for GA airports.
  • Hangar rentals: Monthly rent for airport-owned hangar space.
  • Ground lease payments: Rent for land where tenants build their own improvements.
  • Tie-down fees: For outdoor aircraft parking.
  • Landing fees: Per-landing or per-thousand-pounds charge. Common at commercial airports, less common at GA airports.
  • FBO percentage rent: Some airports charge a percentage of FBO gross revenue in addition to or instead of base rent.

Non-Aeronautical Revenue:

  • Terminal concessions: Food, retail, advertising (commercial airports)
  • Parking: Can be a significant revenue generator at commercial airports
  • Rental car CFCs (Customer Facility Charges): Fund rental car facilities
  • Agricultural leases: For non-aviation use of airport land
  • Cell tower leases: Lease space for communication towers (if compatible with operations)

Government Funding:

  • AIP grants: Typically 90% federal / 10% local match (or 90/5/5 with state participation)
  • BIL supplemental funding: Additional infrastructure dollars through 2026
  • State aviation grants: Vary widely by state
  • PFC (Passenger Facility Charges): Up to $4.50/enplaned passenger for FAA-approved projects (commercial service airports only)

Revenue Diversion -- What It Is and How to Avoid It

▼

Revenue diversion is using airport revenue for non-airport purposes. The FAA takes this extremely seriously. It's a violation of Grant Assurance #25 and can result in loss of AIP funding, enforcement action, and payback requirements.

Common forms of revenue diversion:

  • Transferring airport revenue to the city/county general fund
  • Charging the airport excessive fees for city services (police, fire, admin) beyond what's actually provided
  • Using airport land for non-airport purposes without fair compensation
  • Requiring the airport to pay for non-airport infrastructure (roads, utilities) that primarily benefit others
  • Not crediting the airport for property tax revenue generated by airport property

What IS allowed:

  • Reasonable charges for actual services provided to the airport by the sponsor
  • Reimbursement of actual costs for police, fire, and administrative services attributable to the airport
  • A reasonable rate of return on city-funded airport capital improvements
Critical Warning

The FAA has ordered airports to pay back millions in diverted revenue. If your city council is taking airport funds for non-airport purposes, you need to address this with your FAA ADO immediately. The longer it goes on, the larger the payback obligation. Consult aviation counsel.

Operating Budget Basics

▼

Your operating budget should cover:

  • Revenue projections by source (be conservative)
  • Personnel costs: Salaries, benefits, training (usually the largest expense)
  • Utilities: Electric (especially for airfield lighting), water, gas, communications
  • Maintenance: Airfield, buildings, equipment, vehicles
  • Insurance: Airport liability, property, vehicles, workers' comp
  • Professional services: Legal, accounting, consulting
  • Supplies and equipment: Pavement materials, signs, paint, FOD equipment
  • Capital reserves: Set aside funds for the local match on future grant projects
  • Debt service: Bond payments, loans
Pro Tip

Build a reserve fund equal to at least 3-6 months of operating expenses. When a lighting transformer blows at 2 AM or a freak storm damages a hangar, you need cash available immediately. Don't count on being able to get emergency grant funding quickly.

Capital Improvement Planning

▼

Your CIP is your airport's 5-year investment roadmap. It should be:

  • Prioritized: Safety first, then regulatory compliance, then preservation, then capacity/new development
  • Realistic: Don't list $50M in projects if you get $1M/year in entitlement funds
  • Updated annually: Roll it forward each year and adjust as conditions change
  • Coordinated: Align with your master plan, ALP, and state aviation system plan

Rate-Setting & Justifying Rate Increases

▼

Raising rates is never popular, but it's often necessary. How to do it right:

  1. Establish a methodology. CPI-based annual adjustments are defensible and predictable. Include escalation clauses in all new leases.
  2. Benchmark against peers. Survey 5-10 similar airports in your region. If you're significantly below market, you have data to support an increase.
  3. Show the math. "Our costs have increased X% while rates have been flat for Y years. Here's the gap."
  4. Phase it in. A 50% increase in one year is harder to accept than 10% per year for 5 years. Give tenants time to adjust.
  5. Communicate early. No one likes surprises. Discuss upcoming rate changes at tenant meetings before they show up in lease amendments.
  6. Document the self-sustaining requirement. Grant Assurance #24 requires you to charge rates that make the airport as self-sustaining as possible. You're not raising rates because you want to -- you're obligated to.
Pro Tip

The best time to establish fair rates is in new leases. It's much easier to set the right rate from the start than to bring an existing below-market lease up to market. When leases come up for renewal, that's your opportunity to reset.

When to Hire a Financial Consultant

▼

Consider bringing in an airport financial consultant when:

  • You need to develop or update your rate and charges methodology
  • Revenue diversion questions have been raised
  • You're negotiating a complex lease (especially FBO or major development)
  • Your airport needs to go from subsidy to self-sustaining
  • You're considering PFCs or bond financing
  • A tenant is challenging your rates as unfair
  • You need an economic impact study
Pro Tip

An airport financial consultant can pay for themselves quickly. One firm found that a medium-sized GA airport was undercharging on ground leases by $200K annually. The $30K study paid for itself in less than two months of corrected rates.

Benchmarking Against Peer Airports

▼

Identify 5-10 airports similar to yours in size, region, and activity level. Compare:

  • Hangar rental rates per square foot
  • Ground lease rates per square foot
  • Fuel flowage fees
  • Tie-down fees
  • Landing fees (if applicable)
  • Total operating revenue per based aircraft
  • Operating expense per operation
  • Staffing levels

Sources for data: FAA Form 5010 (Airport Master Record), AOPA airport database, state aviation directories, and simply calling peer airport managers (most are happy to share).

Pro Tip

Join your state airport managers association and attend AAAE conferences. The peer network you build is invaluable. When you need to know how another airport handles a specific issue, having a colleague you can call is worth more than any consultant.

Emergency Preparedness

Plans, exercises, and response -- because emergencies don't wait for perfect conditions

Airport Emergency Plan (AEP) Basics

▼

Your AEP is required for Part 139 airports but is a best practice for all airports. It should address:

  • Aircraft incidents and accidents on and off the airport
  • Structural fires
  • Natural disasters (tornado, flood, earthquake, hurricane)
  • Hazardous materials incidents (fuel spills, chemical releases)
  • Bomb threats / security incidents
  • Medical emergencies
  • Power outages (especially affecting airfield lighting)
  • Water rescue (if applicable)

The plan should identify roles, responsibilities, communication procedures, resource locations, and mutual aid agreements. It must be reviewed annually and exercised on the required schedule.

Pro Tip

Your AEP is only as good as the people who know it. Don't let it sit on a shelf. Conduct an annual tabletop exercise at minimum, even if you're not Part 139. Walking through a scenario around a conference table takes half a day and reveals gaps you didn't know existed.

Emergency Exercise Requirements

▼

For Part 139 airports, emergency exercises are required on a specific cycle:

  • Full-scale exercise: Every 36 months (triennial). Involves actual deployment of resources and simulated casualties. This is the big one -- coordinate with local fire, police, EMS, hospitals, Red Cross, NTSB, and FAA.
  • Tabletop exercise: In the years between full-scale exercises. Walk through a scenario around a table, discussing roles, responsibilities, and decision points.

Tips for effective exercises:

  • Make it realistic. Use scenarios that could actually happen at your airport.
  • Involve all stakeholders -- not just airport staff.
  • Appoint evaluators/observers separate from participants.
  • Conduct a thorough after-action review (AAR). Be honest about what didn't work.
  • Document corrective actions and follow through on them.
Pro Tip

Do a "no-notice" exercise once in a while -- call a simulated alert without advance warning and see how your team responds. The difference between a planned exercise and a surprise drill will tell you how prepared you really are.

Mutual Aid Agreements

▼

No airport can handle a major emergency alone. Establish written mutual aid agreements with:

  • Local fire departments (structural and hazmat capability)
  • Law enforcement agencies
  • Emergency medical services and hospitals
  • Neighboring airports
  • Red Cross / emergency management agency
  • Military installations (if nearby)
  • Towing / recovery companies for aircraft

Review and update agreements annually. Make sure contact information is current and that mutual aid partners understand airport access procedures and airfield safety.

Media Relations During Emergencies

▼

When an incident happens, the media will arrive quickly. Be prepared:

  • Designate a spokesperson. Only one person talks to the media. Everyone else refers inquiries to them.
  • Establish a media staging area away from the incident site but close enough to be useful.
  • Stick to the facts. Only confirm what you know. "The investigation is ongoing" is a perfectly acceptable answer.
  • Show compassion. If there are injuries or fatalities, express concern and empathy before talking about operations.
  • Don't speculate. Cause, blame, and investigation findings are for the NTSB and FAA, not you.
  • Have a holding statement ready. "We are aware of an incident at [airport name]. Emergency response is underway. We will provide an update when more information is available."

[If known: X people have been transported to area hospitals / there are no reported injuries at this time.] The airport [is/is not] currently operational. Fuel Spill Response Procedures

▼

Fuel spills happen. Your response determines whether it's a minor cleanup or a major environmental incident.

  1. Contain: Use absorbent booms and pads to prevent the spill from spreading, especially toward storm drains, waterways, or soil.
  2. Stop the source: Shut off the fuel supply if safe to do so. Don't take risks with ignition sources.
  3. Notify: Alert your fire department if the spill is significant. Report to your state environmental agency as required (know your state's reporting thresholds).
  4. Clean up: Absorb the fuel, collect contaminated materials, and dispose of properly (hazardous waste).
  5. Document: Photos, cause analysis, quantities, cleanup actions, and disposal records.
  6. Report: Update your SPCC plan if the spill reveals a deficiency.
Pro Tip

Keep spill kits staged at every fuel storage and fueling area. Make sure your staff and FBO employees know where they are and how to use them. Do spill response drills at least twice a year. The first 5 minutes of a spill response determine whether it's a $500 cleanup or a $50,000 remediation.

Wildlife Strike Reporting

▼

Report all wildlife strikes using FAA Form 5200-7, available online at the FAA Wildlife Strike Database. Report even if:

  • No damage resulted
  • The pilot isn't sure if something was hit
  • Only remains are found on the runway (report as a strike)

Collect and preserve remains when possible -- the Smithsonian Institution's Feather Identification Lab can identify species from even small feather samples. This data helps build your wildlife hazard management program.

Every dollar you spend on preventive maintenance saves $4-6 in future rehabilitation costs. A $2/SY crack seal done on time can prevent a $60/SY reconstruction done 10 years too early. The pavement deterioration curve is steep once you fall behind -- don't fall behind.

▼

PCI is a 0-100 scale rating of pavement condition based on a visual survey of distresses (cracks, weathering, rutting, patches, etc.):

Get a PCI survey done every 3 years. It's AIP-eligible and essential for planning your CIP. Most state DOTs have pavement management programs that can help.

Pro Tip

The critical range is PCI 55-70. This is where the right treatment at the right time can extend pavement life by 10-15 years. Below 40, you're essentially in reconstruction territory, which is 5-10x more expensive. Use PCI data to convince your board that maintenance funding now saves millions later.

Airfield Marking Standards

▼

FAA AC 150/5340-1 (current edition) is your bible for airfield markings. Key points:

  • White markings: Runways (centerline, threshold, aiming point, touchdown zone, edge markings)
  • Yellow markings: Taxiways, aprons, hold positions, closed markings
  • Marking materials: Paint or thermoplastic. Paint is cheaper but less durable. Waterborne paint is replacing solvent-based for environmental reasons.
  • Reflectivity: Glass beads are applied to wet paint for nighttime visibility. Test reflectivity annually.
  • Runway hold position markings: The most critical marking on the airfield. These must be highly visible at all times.
Pro Tip

Budget for full airfield restriping every 2-3 years for high-traffic runways. In between, touch up hold position markings, centerlines, and edge lines annually. Faded markings are a safety issue and will be noted in any FAA inspection.

Lighting Maintenance & PAPI/REIL Basics

▼

Airfield lighting requires regular maintenance for safety:

  • PAPI (Precision Approach Path Indicator): Provides visual glide slope guidance. Must be flight-checked after installation and anytime the alignment is disturbed. Check aim annually.
  • REIL (Runway End Identifier Lights): Synchronized flashing lights at the runway threshold. Replace bulbs proactively -- a dark REIL on a dark night can make finding the runway very difficult.
  • Edge lights: Inspect monthly for burned-out units. Replace before you reach 10% outage.
  • Regulators and transformers: The heart of your lighting system. Monitor for overheating and schedule preventive maintenance per manufacturer recommendations.
  • LED conversion: Dramatically reduces maintenance and energy costs. AIP-eligible. If you're still running incandescent, budget for LED conversion.
Pro Tip

Keep a complete set of spare lamps, transformers, and connectors in stock. When a runway edge light circuit fails at sunset, you need to fix it or NOTAM it immediately. Having parts on the shelf versus waiting for a delivery can mean the difference between a one-hour fix and a multi-day closure.

Snow and Ice Control

▼

If you're at a northern airport, winter operations may be your biggest challenge. Key elements:

  • Snow and Ice Control Plan (SICP): Required for Part 139 airports. Covers procedures, priorities, equipment, chemicals, and NOTAM reporting.
  • Priority order: Active runway(s) first, then taxiways to/from active runway, then apron areas, then secondary surfaces.
  • SRE (Snow Removal Equipment): Plows, brooms, blowers, and chemical spreaders. Size and quantity based on your airport's surfaces and storm expectations.
  • Deicing chemicals: Potassium acetate, sodium acetate, urea. Know your chemicals, their effective temperature range, and environmental impacts.
  • NOTAM reporting: Report pavement conditions using the TALPA/ARC (Takeoff and Landing Performance Assessment / Airport Runway Condition) format. Accuracy is critical -- pilots make go/no-go decisions based on your reports.
Pro Tip

Pre-treat before the storm. Applying chemical before snow/ice accumulates prevents bonding and makes mechanical removal much more effective. And maintain your equipment in the fall -- a broken plow at 3 AM during a blizzard is not the time to discover a hydraulic leak.

FOD Prevention Program

▼

FOD (Foreign Object Debris/Damage) is anything on the airfield that doesn't belong there. It can damage aircraft, cause flat tires, or worse. Prevention strategies:

  • Daily airfield inspections with specific attention to FOD
  • FOD containers at vehicle access points and near hangars
  • Require contractors to police their work areas daily
  • Secure vehicle loads entering the airfield
  • Conduct periodic FOD walks (great for awareness)
  • Maintain pavement edges and shoulders to prevent gravel migration
  • Ensure all vehicles on the airfield have proper tire pressure and secure loads
Pro Tip

FOD damage to a jet engine can cost $500K+. A simple FOD walk costs nothing. Make FOD awareness part of your airport culture. Consider a FOD reporting system where anyone -- staff, pilots, tenants -- can report FOD and be recognized for it.

Vegetation Management

▼
  • Mowing schedule: Keep grass in safety areas at 6-12 inches. Too short attracts certain birds (geese love short grass). Too tall obscures markings and lights.
  • Tree management: Monitor trees in approach and transitional surfaces. If they penetrate Part 77 surfaces, they need to be trimmed or removed.
  • Weed control: Especially important around pavement edges where weeds can accelerate pavement deterioration.
  • Seed selection: Choose grass species that are less attractive to wildlife. Your USDA Wildlife Services contact can advise.

Environmental

NEPA, stormwater, PFAS, and the growing environmental responsibilities of airport management

NEPA Process for Airport Projects

▼

The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) requires environmental review for federally funded projects. Three levels:

1. Categorical Exclusion (CE / CatEx)
For actions that normally don't significantly affect the environment. This is the simplest and fastest path. Examples of airport CEs:

  • Pavement rehabilitation (same footprint)
  • Lighting upgrades
  • Equipment purchases
  • Minor facility renovations
  • Marking and signage improvements

2. Environmental Assessment (EA)
For actions that may have significant impacts but probably don't. Results in either a Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI) or a requirement for a full EIS. Examples:

  • Runway extensions
  • New taxiways or aprons in previously undeveloped areas
  • Land acquisition
  • Actions affecting wetlands, floodplains, or historic properties

3. Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)
For major actions with significant environmental impacts. Rare for most airport projects, common for major capacity projects at large airports. Takes 2-5+ years and costs millions.

Pro Tip

Start the NEPA process early -- it's almost always on the critical path. An EA can take 12-24 months. If your consultant doesn't bring up environmental review early in the planning process, ask about it. Nothing delays a project like discovering at 90% design that you need an EA you didn't budget for.

Stormwater Management (NPDES/SPCC)

▼

Airports have extensive impervious surfaces and handle petroleum products, making stormwater management critical.

NPDES (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System):

  • If your airport discharges stormwater to waters of the U.S., you likely need an NPDES permit
  • Construction activities disturbing 1+ acres require a construction stormwater permit and SWPPP (Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan)
  • Industrial activities (including deicing) may trigger industrial stormwater permit requirements

SPCC (Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure) Plan:

  • Required if you store more than 1,320 gallons of oil aboveground or 42,000 gallons underground in aggregate
  • Must be prepared by a Professional Engineer
  • Reviewed and updated every 5 years or after a spill
  • Requires secondary containment for fuel storage tanks

PFAS -- The Emerging Crisis

▼

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) -- often called "forever chemicals" -- may be the biggest environmental challenge facing airports in the coming decades.

Why airports are affected: AFFF (Aqueous Film Forming Foam) used for aircraft firefighting contains PFAS. Decades of use in training and emergencies has contaminated soil and groundwater at airports across the country.

Current regulatory landscape:

  • EPA has established drinking water standards for several PFAS compounds at very low levels (parts per trillion)
  • Many states have adopted even stricter standards
  • PFAS are being considered for Superfund/CERCLA liability designation
  • FAA is transitioning to fluorine-free foam alternatives for Part 139 airports

What you should be doing now:

  • Inventory all current and historical AFFF use areas (training pads, hangars, storage)
  • Consider preliminary environmental site assessments for PFAS
  • Transition to fluorine-free foam as FAA approves alternatives
  • Properly dispose of remaining PFAS-containing AFFF (it's hazardous waste in many states)
  • Monitor EPA and state regulatory developments closely
  • Document everything -- dates, quantities, locations of all AFFF use
Critical Warning

PFAS remediation can cost millions. If your airport has used AFFF historically (and most Part 139 airports have), this is not a matter of "if" but "when" you'll need to address it. Start planning now. Consult with environmental counsel about your potential liability exposure. The airports that are proactive about PFAS will be in far better shape than those that wait for enforcement action.

Pro Tip

Track the FAA's research on fluorine-free foam alternatives. When they certify new products, be an early adopter. Not only does it stop adding to the problem, but it demonstrates good faith to regulators and potentially limits future liability.

Deicing Fluid Management

▼

Aircraft deicing fluid (primarily propylene glycol or ethylene glycol) has a high biological oxygen demand (BOD) that can harm waterways if discharged untreated.

  • Collect spent deicing fluid where possible (glycol recovery systems)
  • Monitor stormwater for glycol concentrations during deicing season
  • Your NPDES permit likely has glycol-related discharge limits
  • Consider pavement deicers carefully -- some have environmental impacts
  • Recycle/reprocess collected glycol where feasible

Wetlands, Endangered Species, and Other Environmental Constraints

▼

Airport projects frequently encounter environmental constraints:

Wetlands: If your project impacts wetlands, you'll need a Section 404 permit from the Army Corps of Engineers and likely wetland mitigation (creating or restoring wetlands elsewhere). This adds time and cost -- account for it in planning.

Endangered Species: The Endangered Species Act (ESA) applies to all federal actions. If protected species are present, consultation with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service is required. This can significantly constrain construction timing and methods.

Historic Resources: Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act requires consideration of impacts to historic properties. Archaeological surveys may be needed for ground-disturbing work.

Environmental Justice: Executive Order 12898 requires consideration of disproportionate impacts on minority and low-income communities. This is increasingly important in NEPA reviews, especially for noise and air quality impacts.

Pro Tip

Environmental constraints rarely get less complex over time. The best approach is to identify all environmental issues early in the planning process and build the necessary studies and permits into your project schedule and budget. A surprise wetland delineation at 60% design can delay your project by a year.
