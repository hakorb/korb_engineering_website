Welcome to DFW. You're inheriting 7 runways, 5 terminals, ~17,000 acres, and ~22 million annual operations — one of the busiest airfields on earth. Your first 30 days set the tone for whether your crew trusts you and whether FAA, airline, and executive leadership see you as a steady hand.

This playbook is structured day-by-day. Work through it in order. Every day has tasks (REVIEW / MEET / INSPECT / DOCUMENT / PLAN), pitfalls to avoid, and tips from people who've done this before.

Your goal at the end of day 30: you have walked every runway, met every direct report, read the Part 139 ACM cover-to-cover, and produced a written 90-day plan.

[ P04 ] TOP PITFALLS TO AVOID (ALL 30 DAYS)
1. Running off without meeting the crew. Your pavement crew and grounds crew have decades of institutional knowledge. Blow past the introductions and you'll burn political capital that takes months to rebuild. Meet every direct report 1-on-1 before suggesting a single change.
2. Committing to a policy change in the first 30 days. You don't know the system yet. Every policy has a reason, often an incident that led to it. Default answer: "I want to understand why we do it this way first."
3. Missing Part 139 self-inspection items. DFW self-inspects three times daily plus after weather events. Review the current form and ride along at least once before week 2. Inspection items drive your budget.
4. Ignoring the PCI database. Every panel and section has a PCI score and an M&R history. Don't propose crack sealing before reading the most recent pavement management report.
5. Under-estimating wildlife hazard. Grounds decisions (grass height, drainage, ponding, tree line) feed wildlife attractants. Your turf spec directly affects bird strikes. Get the USDA Wildlife Hazard Assessment and read it.
6. Surprising the airfield ops team. Anything you do on the airfield has to be coordinated through Airfield Operations and ATC. Never show up at a runway closure without a NOTAM, an escort vehicle, and a radio.
7. Over-promising the capital plan. The 5-year capital plan exists. Learn it before you suggest a $15M rehab that's actually programmed for year 4.
8. Forgetting that DFW is dual-owned. Fort Worth & Dallas share ownership; the airport board is the operator. Get clear on escalation: what goes to the board, what stays internal, what is city business.
