    General Industry Standards — 29 CFR 1910

    OSHA's General Industry standards apply to all employers and employees in general industry workplaces. These cover the baseline safety requirements every facility must meet.

    29 CFR 1910.22 – 1910.30
    Walking-Working Surfaces
    Covers all permanent places of employment except where only domestic, mining, or agricultural work is performed. Includes floors, platforms, aisles, stairways, ladders, and scaffolds.

    Key Requirements

    • All walking-working surfaces must be kept clean, orderly, and sanitary (1910.22(a))
    • Floors must be free of hazards like sharp protruding objects, loose boards, and holes (1910.22(a)(3))
    • Unprotected sides and edges of walking-working surfaces 4 feet or more above a lower level require guardrail systems, safety net systems, or personal fall arrest systems (1910.28)
    • Fixed ladders over 24 feet must have ladder safety systems or personal fall arrest systems (1910.28(b)(9))
    • Employers must train workers on fall hazards and protection systems (1910.30)
    • Stairway landings must be at least as wide as the stair and at least 30 inches deep (1910.25(b))
    Common Violations: Missing guardrails on elevated platforms, uneven floor surfaces not repaired, poor housekeeping creating slip/trip hazards, ladders used improperly or in poor condition.
    Penalties (FY2025): Serious: up to $16,550 per violation. Willful/Repeat: up to $165,514 per violation.
    Application Tips: Conduct monthly walking surface inspections. Document all housekeeping activities. Replace damaged floor plates immediately. Mark temporary floor holes with barricades and warning signs.
    29 CFR 1910.33 – 1910.39
    Means of Egress / Emergency Action Plans
    Requirements for exit routes, emergency action plans, and fire prevention plans in all general industry workplaces.

    Key Requirements

    • Every workplace must have at least two exit routes that are remote from each other (1910.36(b))
    • Exit routes must be permanent, maintained during construction/repairs, and clearly marked (1910.37)
    • Exit signs must be illuminated with "EXIT" in letters at least 6 inches high (1910.37(b))
    • Exit doors must open in the direction of travel when serving 50+ people (1910.36(d)(1))
    • An Emergency Action Plan (EAP) is required when any OSHA standard mandates one (1910.38)
    • EAP must include evacuation procedures, reporting procedures, and employee alarm systems
    Common Violations: Blocked or locked exit doors, missing/burned-out exit signs, no written EAP, failure to train employees on evacuation routes.
    Penalties (FY2025): Serious: up to $16,550 per violation. Failure to abate: up to $16,550 per day.
    Application Tips: Walk exit routes quarterly. Test exit lighting monthly. Post evacuation maps at key locations. Conduct evacuation drills at least annually.
    29 CFR 1910.301 – 1910.399
    Electrical
    Design safety standards for electrical systems and safety-related work practices for employees working on or near exposed energized and de-energized parts.

    Key Requirements

    • Wiring design and protection must comply with NEC/NFPA 70 standards (Subpart S)
    • All electrical equipment must be free from recognized hazards (1910.303(b))
    • Working clearances around electrical panels: minimum 30 inches wide, 36 inches deep for 0-150V (1910.303(g))
    • GFCI protection required for all 120V, 15- and 20-amp receptacles in wet/damp locations (1910.304(b)(3))
    • Lockout/tagout procedures required for servicing energized equipment (1910.333(b))
    • Only qualified persons may work on energized parts (1910.332)
    Common Violations: Blocked electrical panels, missing covers on junction boxes, improper use of extension cords as permanent wiring, lack of GFCI protection, no LOTO procedures.
    Penalties (FY2025): Serious: up to $16,550. This is consistently a Top 10 OSHA violation category.
    Application Tips: Maintain 36-inch clearance at all panels. Label all breakers. Replace damaged cords immediately. Train all employees on basic electrical hazard awareness.
    29 CFR 1910.132 – 1910.138
    Personal Protective Equipment (General Industry)
    Employer requirements for hazard assessment, PPE selection, training, and maintenance for general industry workplaces.

    Key Requirements

    • Employers must perform a written hazard assessment to determine required PPE (1910.132(d))
    • Employers must provide PPE at no cost to employees (1910.132(h)) — exceptions: safety-toe shoes, prescription safety eyewear, logging boots, everyday clothing
    • Eye/face protection must meet ANSI Z87.1 (1910.133)
    • Respiratory protection requires a written program per 1910.134
    • Head protection must meet ANSI Z89.1 (1910.135)
    • Training required before use; retraining when PPE changes or employee shows lack of understanding (1910.132(f))
    Common Violations: No written hazard assessment, employees not trained on PPE use, PPE not inspected before use, wrong PPE for the hazard.
    Penalties (FY2025): Serious: up to $16,550. PPE violations are consistently in OSHA's Top 10 most cited standards.
    Application Tips: Certify hazard assessments with date, assessor name, and workplace evaluated. Re-assess when new processes or equipment are introduced. Keep PPE training records for at least 3 years.
    29 CFR 1910.155 – 1910.165
    Fire Protection
    Requirements for fire brigades, portable fire extinguishers, fixed suppression systems, fire detection, and employee alarm systems.

    Key Requirements

    • Portable fire extinguishers must be provided, mounted, located, and identified (1910.157)
    • Extinguishers must be inspected monthly and receive annual maintenance (1910.157(e))
    • Travel distance: 75 feet max for Class A hazards, 50 feet max for Class B (1910.157(d))
    • Employee training on extinguisher use required upon initial assignment and annually (1910.157(g))
    • If employer provides portable extinguishers, they must also provide education (1910.157(g))
    • Fire detection systems must be maintained in operable condition (1910.164)
    Common Violations: Expired/missing extinguisher inspections, blocked access to extinguishers, inadequate extinguisher size for hazard class, no employee fire training.
    Application Tips: Tag all extinguishers with monthly inspection dates. Use PASS method training: Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep. Map extinguisher locations on floor plans.
    29 CFR 1910.176 – 1910.184
    Materials Handling & Storage
    Safe practices for handling, storing, and disposing of materials including powered industrial trucks (forklifts), cranes, derricks, and slings.

    Key Requirements

    • Storage areas must be kept free of accumulation of materials that create tripping, fire, or pest hazards (1910.176(c))
    • Powered industrial truck operators must be trained and evaluated (1910.178(l))
    • Forklift operator evaluations required every 3 years (1910.178(l)(4)(iii))
    • Overhead crane inspections: frequent (daily/monthly) and periodic (1-12 months) per 1910.179(j)
    • Slings must be inspected before each use and removed from service if defective (1910.184(d))
    Common Violations: Untrained forklift operators, overloaded slings, no daily crane inspections, improper stacking of materials.
    Application Tips: Keep forklift certification records readily available. Post load capacity on all cranes. Use tag lines on suspended loads. Inspect slings per manufacturer specs.
    29 CFR 1910.211 – 1910.219
    Machine Guarding
    Requirements for guarding machines to protect operators and other employees from hazards created by point of operation, ingoing nip points, rotating parts, flying chips, and sparks.

    Key Requirements

    • One or more guarding methods required at point of operation where employee exposure exists (1910.212(a))
    • Guards must prevent hands, arms, and other body parts from contacting dangerous parts (1910.212(a)(2))
    • Anchored fixed machinery must prevent walking into moving parts (1910.212(a)(5))
    • Abrasive wheel machinery: tongue guard within 1/4 inch of wheel, tool rest within 1/8 inch (1910.215(b))
    • All pulleys, belts, flywheels, shafting must be guarded if within 7 feet of floor (1910.219)
    Common Violations: Missing guards, guards removed and not replaced, bypassed interlocks, inadequate point-of-operation guarding. Machine guarding is perennially in OSHA's Top 10.
    Application Tips: Never operate equipment with guards removed. Conduct quarterly guard inspections. Train operators to verify guards before startup. Use LOTO before adjusting guards.
    29 CFR 1910.251 – 1910.255
    Welding, Cutting & Brazing
    Fire prevention, ventilation, and protective equipment requirements for gas welding/cutting, arc welding, and resistance welding operations.

    Key Requirements

    • Fire watchers required when welding within 35 feet of combustibles that cannot be moved or shielded (1910.252(a)(2)(iii))
    • Compressed gas cylinders must be secured upright and valve caps in place during transport/storage (1910.253(b))
    • Arc welding: electrode holders must be fully insulated; cables must be in good condition (1910.254)
    • Proper ventilation required in confined or enclosed spaces; mechanical ventilation when ceiling is less than 16 feet (1910.252(c))
    • Welders must use proper shade filter lenses — shade 10-14 for arc welding (1910.252(b))
    Common Violations: No hot work permits, missing fire watchers, damaged welding leads, cylinders not secured, no ventilation in confined areas.
    Application Tips: Always use hot work permit system. Inspect welding leads daily. Keep a 20-lb ABC fire extinguisher within reach. Post-weld fire watch for minimum 30 minutes.
    29 CFR 1910.1000 – 1910.1450
    Toxic & Hazardous Substances
    Permissible Exposure Limits (PELs), substance-specific standards (asbestos, lead, benzene, etc.), Hazard Communication (HazCom), and occupational exposure to hazardous chemicals in laboratories.

    Key Requirements

    • Hazard Communication (HazCom/GHS) program required with written plan, SDS, labels, and training (1910.1200)
    • All chemical containers must be labeled with product identifier, signal word, hazard statements, pictograms (1910.1200(f))
    • Safety Data Sheets (SDS) must be readily accessible to employees during shifts (1910.1200(g))
    • Employee exposure to airborne contaminants must not exceed PELs in Table Z-1, Z-2, Z-3 (1910.1000)
    • Specific standards for asbestos (1910.1001), lead (1910.1025), benzene (1910.1028) with lower PELs and medical surveillance
    • Laboratories must have a Chemical Hygiene Plan (1910.1450)
    Common Violations: No written HazCom program, missing/outdated SDS, unlabeled secondary containers, no employee training on new chemicals. HazCom is the #1 most cited OSHA standard.
    Application Tips: Maintain a chemical inventory and update annually. Train workers on SDS 16-section format. Use GHS-compliant labels on all secondary containers. Keep SDS binder (or digital access) in each work area.
    29 CFR 1910.146
    Permit-Required Confined Spaces
    Requirements for practices and procedures to protect general industry employees from the hazards of entry into permit-required confined spaces.

    Key Requirements

    • Employer must evaluate workplace to identify all permit-required confined spaces (1910.146(c))
    • Danger signs required: "DANGER — PERMIT-REQUIRED CONFINED SPACE, DO NOT ENTER" (1910.146(c)(2))
    • Written permit space program required before entry (1910.146(d))
    • Atmospheric testing required before and during entry: O2 (19.5-23.5%), LEL (<10%), toxic substances (1910.146(d)(5))
    • Attendant must remain outside the space at all times during entry (1910.146(d)(6))
    • Rescue provisions must be in place before entry begins (1910.146(d)(9))
    Common Violations: No space evaluation, entry without permits, no atmospheric testing, no attendant posted, inadequate rescue planning.
    Penalties (FY2025): Confined space fatalities often result in willful citations up to $165,514 per violation.
    Application Tips: Maintain a confined space inventory with maps. Calibrate atmospheric monitors before each use. Practice rescue procedures annually. Never rely on "sniff tests" for atmosphere.
    29 CFR 1910.147
    Control of Hazardous Energy (Lockout/Tagout)
    Procedures to prevent unexpected energization or startup of machines and equipment, or release of stored energy during servicing and maintenance.

    Key Requirements

    • Written energy control program with machine-specific procedures (1910.147(c)(4))
    • Each authorized employee must apply their own lock and tag (1910.147(c)(5))
    • Six-step LOTO sequence: prepare, shutdown, isolation, lockout/tagout, stored energy, verification (1910.147(d))
    • Periodic inspections of procedures at least annually (1910.147(c)(6))
    • Training for authorized, affected, and other employees (1910.147(c)(7))
    • Group lockout procedures required when multiple employees service the same equipment (1910.147(f)(3))
    Common Violations: No written procedures, no periodic inspections, failure to train affected employees, no machine-specific procedures. LOTO is consistently in OSHA's Top 5 most cited.
    Application Tips: Create machine-specific LOTO procedures with photos. Use one-lock-per-person rule. Document annual inspections with certifier name and date. Never use tags alone if lockout is possible.

    Construction Standards — 29 CFR 1926

    OSHA's Construction standards apply to all construction, alteration, and repair work, including painting and decorating. Construction is OSHA's most-cited industry sector.

    29 CFR 1926.501 – 1926.503
    Fall Protection
    Requirements for fall protection in construction at elevations of 6 feet or more above a lower level. The #1 most-cited OSHA standard overall.

    Key Requirements

    • Fall protection required at 6 feet in construction (vs 4 feet in general industry) (1926.501(b)(1))
    • Guardrails: top rail at 42 inches (+/- 3 inches), mid rail, capable of 200 lbs force (1926.502(b))
    • Personal fall arrest systems: 5,000 lb anchorage per employee, or designed by a qualified person (1926.502(d))
    • Safety nets must extend 8-13 feet beyond the edge depending on fall distance (1926.502(c))
    • Holes (2 inches or greater) in walking/working surfaces must be covered or guarded (1926.502(i))
    • Fall protection plan allowed only when conventional methods are infeasible (1926.502(k))
    • Written training required for each employee exposed to fall hazards (1926.503)
    Common Violations: No fall protection at 6+ feet, inadequate guardrails, unprotected floor holes, untrained workers, fall protection plan used when conventional methods are feasible. Fall Protection has been the #1 most-cited standard for 14 consecutive years.
    Penalties (FY2025): Serious: up to $16,550. Willful: up to $165,514. Multiple instance citations are common on jobsites.
    Application Tips: 100% tie-off policy. Inspect harnesses and lanyards before each use. Mark all floor/roof openings immediately. Include fall protection in every pre-task plan.
    29 CFR 1926.450 – 1926.454
    Scaffolding
    Requirements for the design, construction, and use of scaffolding in construction operations.

    Key Requirements

    • Scaffolds must support 4x the maximum intended load without failure (1926.451(a)(1))
    • Scaffold platforms must be at least 18 inches wide and fully decked (1926.451(b))
    • Guardrails required on all open sides/ends 10 feet or more above lower level (1926.451(g))
    • Cross bracing is NOT acceptable as a top rail (1926.451(g)(4)(xv))
    • Access required when scaffold platform is more than 2 feet above/below point of access (1926.451(e))
    • Competent person must inspect scaffolding before each shift and after any event that could affect integrity (1926.451(f)(3))
    • Scaffold erection, modification, and dismantling must be supervised by a competent person (1926.451(f)(7))
    Common Violations: Inadequate planking, missing guardrails, no access ladders, scaffold not inspected daily, erected on unstable base, no competent person supervision. Scaffolding is typically in OSHA's Top 5.
    Application Tips: Use scaffold tags (green = inspected/safe, red = do not use). Train all scaffold users. Never exceed rated load capacity. Plumb and level all frames. Use mud sills on unimproved ground.
    29 CFR 1926.650 – 1926.652
    Excavations
    Requirements for excavation and trenching operations including soil classification, protective systems, access, and hazardous atmospheres.

    Key Requirements

    • Protective system required for excavations 5 feet or deeper (unless excavation is in stable rock) (1926.652(a))
    • Options: sloping, benching, shoring, or shielding based on soil type (1926.652(b)-(c))
    • Soil classification: Type A (cohesive, 1.5 tsf), Type B (0.5 tsf), Type C (0.5 tsf or less) (Appendix A)
    • Maximum allowable slopes: Type A = 3/4:1 (53°), Type B = 1:1 (45°), Type C = 1.5:1 (34°) (Appendix B)
    • Ladder, ramp, or stairway required within 25 feet of lateral travel for trenches 4+ feet deep (1926.651(c))
    • Competent person must inspect excavations daily and after rain, vibration, or other hazard-creating events (1926.651(k))
    • Call 811 before digging — utility locate required (1926.651(b))
    Common Violations: No protective system in trenches 5+ feet, no competent person on site, spoil pile too close to edge (must be 2+ feet back), no means of egress within 25 feet.
    Penalties (FY2025): Trench collapses are frequently fatal. Willful citations up to $165,514 are common. OSHA has a National Emphasis Program on trenching.
    Application Tips: Assume Type C soil unless a competent person classifies otherwise. Keep spoil piles at least 2 feet from the edge. Never enter an unprotected trench even briefly. Consider a trench box for all utility work.
    29 CFR 1926.1400 – 1926.1442
    Cranes & Derricks
    Comprehensive requirements for cranes and derricks used in construction, including operator qualifications, inspections, assembly/disassembly, and power line safety.

    Key Requirements

    • Crane operators must be certified or licensed by an accredited testing organization (1926.1427)
    • Ground conditions must support the crane including outrigger loads (1926.1402)
    • Power line clearance: 20 feet for lines up to 350kV, increased for higher voltages (1926.1408)
    • Annual inspection by a qualified person required; monthly and daily inspections by operator (1926.1412)
    • Load charts must be in the cab and legible (1926.1417(c))
    • Signal person required when operator cannot see load landing area; must be qualified (1926.1419-1422)
    • Assembly/disassembly must be directed by a competent/qualified person (1926.1404)
    Common Violations: Uncertified operators, no pre-shift inspections, inadequate ground preparation, power line encroachment, missing anti-two-block device, exceeding load chart capacity.
    Application Tips: Always verify operator certification cards on site. Complete lift plans for critical picks. Establish power line boundaries with flagging. Never side-load a crane.
    29 CFR 1926.750 – 1926.761
    Steel Erection
    Requirements for steel erection activities including structural steel assembly, columns, beams, open web steel joists, and falling object protection.

    Key Requirements

    • Fall protection required at 15 feet for connectors (exception from 6-foot general rule) (1926.760(b))
    • All other steel erection workers require fall protection at 6 feet (1926.760(a))
    • Controlled Decking Zone (CDZ) allowed for leading-edge decking work at 15-30 feet (1926.760(c))
    • Column anchor bolts must be repaired if damaged (1926.755(a))
    • Four anchor bolts per column minimum (1926.755(a)(1))
    • Perimeter safety cables required at final interior and exterior perimeters (1926.756(e))
    Common Violations: Connectors not using fall protection above 15 feet, no controlled decking zone plan, column splices not secured before release, no perimeter cables.
    Application Tips: Pre-plan steel sequencing with erector. Ensure adequate bolt-up crews follow ironworkers. Verify column plumbness before releasing crane. Use safety cables from day one.
    29 CFR 1926.1050 – 1926.1060
    Stairways & Ladders
    Requirements for all stairways and ladders used in construction. Applies when there is a break in elevation of 19 inches or more and no ramp, runway, or embankment is provided.

    Key Requirements

    • Stairway or ladder required at all points of access where there is a 19-inch elevation change (1926.1051(a))
    • Ladders must extend 3 feet above landing surface (1926.1053(b)(1))
    • Portable ladder duty ratings: Type IA = 300 lb, Type I = 250 lb, Type II = 225 lb, Type III = 200 lb
    • Ladders must be inspected by a competent person; defective ladders tagged "Do Not Use" (1926.1053(b)(15)-(16))
    • Stairrails required on open sides of stairways with 4+ risers or rising 30+ inches (1926.1052(c))
    • Ladder angle: 4:1 ratio (75.5 degrees) for non-self-supporting ladders (1926.1053(b)(5)(i))
    Common Violations: Ladders not extending 3 feet above landing, missing stairrails, using damaged ladders, improper ladder angle, no ladder inspection program. Ladders is typically in OSHA's Top 10.
    Application Tips: Mark the 3-foot extension requirement on all extension ladders. Use ladder levelers on uneven ground. One person on a ladder at a time. Face the ladder when climbing.
    29 CFR 1926.400 – 1926.449
    Electrical (Construction)
    Electrical safety requirements for construction sites including installation, GFCI/AEGP, and work practices near overhead and underground lines.

    Key Requirements

    • GFCI protection required on ALL 120V, 15- and 20-amp receptacles on construction sites (1926.405(a)(2)(ii))
    • Alternative: Assured Equipment Grounding Conductor Program (AEGCP) with written description and testing (1926.404(b)(1)(iii))
    • Minimum clearance from overhead power lines: 10 feet for up to 50kV (1926.416(a))
    • Temporary wiring must be removed immediately upon completion of construction (1926.405(a)(1))
    • Flexible cords must be protected from damage and not used as a substitute for fixed wiring (1926.405(a)(2)(ii)(J))
    Common Violations: No GFCI on temporary receptacles, damaged extension cords in use, working too close to overhead lines, improper grounding, missing covers on boxes and fittings.
    Application Tips: Use portable GFCI devices for all temporary power. Color-code cord inspections quarterly. Identify all overhead lines before mobilizing equipment. Train all workers on 10-foot rule.
    29 CFR 1926.700 – 1926.706
    Concrete & Masonry Construction
    Requirements for concrete and masonry construction including formwork, shoring, precast, lift-slab, and masonry wall bracing.

    Key Requirements

    • No employee permitted beneath concrete buckets during placing operations (1926.701(a))
    • Formwork must be designed, fabricated, erected, supported, braced, and maintained so it can support all loads (1926.703(a))
    • Shoring removal: only when concrete has reached sufficient strength (typically 75% of design) (1926.703(e))
    • Protruding reinforcing steel: guarding required with covers to prevent impalement (1926.701(b))
    • Masonry walls over 8 feet must be braced to prevent overturning (1926.706(a))
    • Post-tensioning: no employees behind jacking equipment during stressing (1926.701(d))
    Common Violations: Unguarded rebar, inadequate formwork shoring, premature shore removal, workers under concrete bucket, masonry walls not braced.
    Application Tips: Use rebar caps on all exposed vertical rebar. Require PE-stamped shoring plans. Test concrete cylinders before removing shores. Brace masonry walls before leaving for the day.

    Aviation-Specific OSHA Considerations

    While FAA regulations (14 CFR) govern aviation operations directly, OSHA standards apply to aviation maintenance, hangar operations, ground support, and airport construction. OSHA and FAA have a Memorandum of Understanding for employee safety on airports.

    OSHA & 14 CFR Part 43
    Aircraft Maintenance Safety
    OSHA general industry standards apply to all aircraft maintenance operations. FAA/OSHA MOU (1975, updated 2000) establishes that OSHA covers worker safety while FAA covers aircraft airworthiness.

    Key Requirements

    • Lockout/tagout (1910.147) applies to aircraft systems during maintenance — de-energize hydraulic, pneumatic, and electrical systems before work
    • Confined space entry (1910.146) applies to fuel tanks, wheel wells, and cargo compartments
    • Fall protection required when working on top of aircraft (wings, fuselage, empennage) — use aircraft-specific docking stands with guardrails
    • Respiratory protection (1910.134) required for fuel tank entry, paint stripping, composite sanding, and chemical cleaning
    • Hazard Communication (1910.1200) for all chemicals: solvents, sealants, hydraulic fluids, MEK, paints, degreasers
    • Hearing protection required in areas exceeding 85 dBA TWA (1910.95) — common around APUs and ground power units
    Application Tips: Develop aircraft-type-specific maintenance safety procedures. Use aircraft docking systems (Dedienne, SAFE) instead of improvised platforms. Coordinate with airline maintenance control for lockout procedures.
    NFPA 409 / OSHA 1910.106
    Hangar Operations Safety
    Safety requirements for aircraft hangar operations, fire protection, and flammable liquid handling. NFPA 409 (Aircraft Hangars) supplements OSHA standards.

    Key Requirements

    • Hangar classified as Group I (large, can house any aircraft), Group II (not exceeding certain floor area), Group III (small, membrane-covered), or Group IV (membrane, limited to certain aircraft)
    • Fire suppression required: foam systems for Group I, sprinklers for Group II-IV (NFPA 409)
    • Electrical installations within hangar floor areas must be suitable for Class I, Division 2 locations up to 18 inches above floor (NFPA 409)
    • Flammable liquid storage per OSHA 1910.106: maximum 25 gallons outside approved cabinets, 60 gallons per cabinet
    • No open flames or hot work without permit and fire watch in hangar areas
    • Overhead crane operations in hangars must comply with 1910.179
    Application Tips: Post "No Smoking" signs at all hangar entrances. Keep battery-powered equipment in explosion-proof areas below 18 inches. Maintain foam system per NFPA 409 Chapter 6. Ensure hangar doors are operable for emergency egress.
    NFPA 407 / OSHA 1910.106
    Fueling Operations
    Safety requirements for aircraft fueling and defueling operations. NFPA 407 (Aircraft Fuel Servicing) is the primary standard, supplemented by OSHA flammable liquid standards.

    Key Requirements

    • Bonding wire must be connected before fueling begins and remain until fueling is complete (NFPA 407)
    • Dead man controls required on all fueling nozzles (NFPA 407 6.3)
    • No fueling within 50 feet of a running APU or open flame
    • Fuel spill response: stop fueling, eliminate ignition sources, deploy absorbent, notify airport operations
    • Fire extinguisher (minimum 20-B:C) within 100 feet of fueling operation (NFPA 407 7.1)
    • Fueling personnel must be trained on fuel properties, bonding, spill response, and emergency procedures
    Application Tips: Always bond before opening fuel connections. Check for fuel leaks/drips before departing the aircraft. Keep fueling vehicle positioned for quick departure. Know your airport emergency number.
    OSHA 1910.178 / 1926.602
    Ground Support Equipment (GSE) Safety
    Safety for belt loaders, tugs, pushback tractors, lavatory carts, GPU/ASU units, air starts, and de-icing equipment on airport ramps.

    Key Requirements

    • GSE operators must be trained on each type of equipment operated (1910.178(l) applies)
    • Daily pre-use inspection required for all motorized GSE
    • Speed limits on airport ramp: typically 5-15 mph per airport rules (airport tenant agreement)
    • Chocking requirements: wheel chocks required whenever GSE is parked adjacent to aircraft
    • GSE with LOX/nitrogen systems must follow compressed gas cylinder standards (1910.101)
    • Hearing protection required for workers spending extended time near GPU, APU, or engine run areas
    Application Tips: Implement a GSE safety checklist for each shift. Paint GSE travel lanes on the ramp. Use orange cones/flags for clearance zones. Report all GSE-to-aircraft incidents per airline SOPs.
    29 CFR 1910.95
    Noise Exposure on Airport Ramps
    OSHA noise exposure limits and hearing conservation requirements for employees working on airport ramps and in maintenance areas.

    Key Requirements

    • Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL): 90 dBA TWA over 8 hours (1910.95(a))
    • Action Level: 85 dBA TWA triggers hearing conservation program (1910.95(c))
    • Hearing conservation program includes: monitoring, audiometric testing, hearing protectors, training, recordkeeping
    • Typical ramp noise levels: jet engine at idle 80-90 dBA, APU 85-95 dBA, engine run-up 120-140 dBA, pushback 75-85 dBA
    • Double hearing protection (plugs + muffs) recommended for exposure above 100 dBA
    • Annual audiometric testing required for all workers in the hearing conservation program (1910.95(g))
    Application Tips: Provide NRR 25+ earmuffs for all ramp workers. Post noise hazard zones. Limit time in high-noise areas. Use communication headsets with hearing protection built in. Monitor with dosimeters annually.
    29 CFR 1910.146
    Confined Space Entry in Aircraft
    Application of permit-required confined space standards to aircraft fuel tanks, wheel wells, cargo bays, and other enclosed aircraft areas.

    Key Requirements

    • Aircraft integral fuel tanks are permit-required confined spaces (PRCS) — oxygen-deficient atmosphere, fuel vapor, limited entry/exit
    • Atmospheric testing required: O2, LEL, toxic gases before and during entry
    • Ventilation: forced-air ventilation required to maintain safe atmosphere throughout the entry
    • Attendant required outside the entry point at all times
    • Rescue plan: non-entry rescue preferred; coordinate with airport ARFF for standby if needed
    • Training required specific to aircraft confined space hazards (fuel vapors, composite materials, sealant fumes)
    Application Tips: Purge fuel tanks with air for at least 24 hours before entry if possible. Use intrinsically safe lighting and tools inside fuel tanks. Anti-static footwear required. Keep rescue equipment at the entry point.
    29 CFR 1910.28 / 1926.501
    Fall Protection on Aircraft
    Fall protection requirements when working on top of aircraft during maintenance, inspection, cleaning, or de-icing operations.

    Key Requirements

    • General industry: fall protection at 4 feet (1910.28) — most aircraft wing/fuselage work exceeds this height
    • Aircraft maintenance docking stands with integrated guardrails are the preferred engineering control
    • Portable platforms must have guardrails on all open sides (1910.29(b))
    • If guardrails are not feasible (wing-top inspections), personal fall arrest systems required
    • Anchorage: use aircraft-specific anchor points rated for fall arrest; never tie off to aircraft components not rated for the load
    • Travel restraint systems can prevent workers from reaching the edge of wings or fuselage
    Application Tips: Invest in aircraft-type-specific maintenance stands (e.g., SAFE Structure tail docks). Use non-marring guardrail attachments to avoid aircraft damage. Evaluate retractable fall limiters for wing-top work. Include fall hazard analysis in all maintenance task cards.

    Personal Protective Equipment Guide

    PPE is the last line of defense in the Hierarchy of Controls. Proper selection, fit, inspection, and maintenance are critical. Employers must provide most PPE at no cost to employees (1910.132(h)).

    Head Protection (Hard Hats) — ANSI Z89.1 ▶
    Head Protection

    When Required

    • Whenever there is danger of head injury from falling or flying objects
    • When working near exposed electrical conductors
    • On all construction sites (industry standard)

    Types

    • Type I: Top impact protection only
    • Type II: Top and lateral impact protection
    • Class E: Electrical — tested to 20,000V phase to ground
    • Class G: General — tested to 2,200V phase to ground
    • Class C: Conductive — no electrical protection

    Inspection Tips

    • Replace if cracked, dented, or penetrated
    • Replace suspension when frayed, torn, or stretched
    • Do not drill holes, paint, or apply stickers that may hide damage
    • Replace per manufacturer guidelines (typically every 5 years for shell, 1 year for suspension)
    • Do not store in direct sunlight — UV degrades the shell
    Eye & Face Protection — ANSI Z87.1 ▶
    Eye & Face Protection

    When Required

    • Flying particles, molten metal, liquid chemicals, acids, caustic liquids, chemical gases or vapors, or light radiation
    • Grinding, chipping, sanding, hammering, sawing, drilling, pouring chemicals, welding

    Types

    • Safety glasses: Impact protection; side shields required when there is a lateral hazard
    • Goggles: Splash/dust protection; direct or indirect vented
    • Face shields: Full face protection; must be worn WITH safety glasses or goggles (not standalone)
    • Welding helmets: Filter lens shades: SMAW/stick = 10-14, GMAW/MIG = 10-14, GTAW/TIG = 8-14, Oxy-fuel cutting = 3-6

    Inspection Tips

    • Check for scratches, cracks, pitting that impair vision
    • Verify Z87+ marking on lenses and frames
    • Clean with manufacturer-approved solutions
    • Replace when lenses cannot be cleaned clear
    Hearing Protection — 29 CFR 1910.95 ▶
    Hearing Protection

    When Required

    • When 8-hour TWA exposure reaches or exceeds 85 dBA (action level)
    • When feasible engineering and administrative controls cannot reduce noise below PEL of 90 dBA

    Types & NRR (Noise Reduction Rating)

    • Foam earplugs: NRR 25-33; disposable, highest attenuation; must be properly inserted (roll, pull, hold)
    • Pre-molded earplugs: NRR 20-27; reusable, easier insertion
    • Earmuffs: NRR 20-30; easy on/off, good for intermittent noise
    • Canal caps: NRR 15-25; convenient, lower protection
    • Dual protection (plugs + muffs): Add 5 dB to higher NRR; use when TWA exceeds 100 dBA

    OSHA NRR De-rating

    • OSHA method: (NRR - 7) / 2 = estimated actual attenuation
    • Example: NRR 33 earplugs = (33-7)/2 = 13 dB effective reduction
    Respiratory Protection — 29 CFR 1910.134 ▶
    Respiratory Protection

    When Required

    • When employees are exposed to airborne contaminants above PELs
    • When engineering controls are not feasible or during installation of controls
    • During emergencies

    Program Requirements (1910.134)

    • Written respiratory protection program
    • Medical evaluation before fit testing (1910.134(e))
    • Annual fit testing for tight-fitting respirators (1910.134(f))
    • Training on use, limitations, and maintenance (1910.134(k))
    • Program administered by a trained program administrator

    Types

    • N95 filtering facepiece: Particulates only; 95% filter efficiency; does NOT protect against gases/vapors
    • Half-face APR: Particulate and/or gas/vapor cartridges; APF = 10
    • Full-face APR: Higher protection; APF = 50
    • PAPR: Powered air, reduces breathing resistance; APF = 25-1000
    • SAR/SCBA: Supplied air for IDLH or oxygen-deficient atmospheres; APF = 10,000+

    Inspection Tips

    • Inspect before each use: straps, valves, cartridges, seal
    • User seal check every time donned (positive and negative pressure checks)
    • Replace cartridges per change schedule or when breakthrough is detected
    • Clean and store properly after each use
    Hand Protection — 29 CFR 1910.138 ▶
    Hand Protection

    When Required

    • When hands are exposed to hazards: absorption, severe cuts/lacerations, abrasions, punctures, chemical burns, thermal burns, harmful temperature extremes

    Selection Guide

    • Leather: Abrasion, cuts, moderate heat
    • Nitrile: Chemicals, oils, fuels; good puncture resistance
    • Latex: Biological hazards; poor chemical resistance
    • Cut-resistant (ANSI A2-A9): Glass, sheet metal, sharp edges
    • Welding gloves: Heat, sparks, molten metal; gauntlet-style for forearm protection
    • Insulated gloves: Electrical work; rated by voltage class (00 through 4)
    • Chemical-resistant (butyl, Viton): Specific chemical permeation data required

    Key Rule

    • Never wear gloves around rotating machinery — entanglement hazard
    • Match glove to the specific hazard using SDS chemical compatibility data
    Foot Protection — ASTM F2413 ▶
    Foot Protection

    When Required

    • Danger of foot injuries from falling or rolling objects, objects piercing the sole, or electrical hazards

    Types

    • Safety-toe (I/75, C/75): Impact/compression protection; steel, composite, or aluminum toe
    • Metatarsal guards (Mt): Protection for the top of the foot
    • Puncture-resistant (PR): Steel or composite plate in sole
    • EH rated: Electrical hazard protection up to 600V in dry conditions
    • SD/static dissipative: Controls static buildup

    Note

    • Employers may allow employee-owned safety footwear if it meets required standards (one of the PPE cost exceptions under 1910.132(h)(4))
    Body & High-Visibility Protection ▶
    Body & High-Visibility Protection

    When Required

    • Body protection for cuts, heat, chemical splash, radiation, and impacts
    • High-visibility garments required on or near roadways (MUTCD/ANSI 107)

    High-Visibility Classes (ANSI/ISEA 107)

    • Class 1: Low traffic, worker fully visible; allows separation from traffic
    • Class 2: Greater visibility needed; work activities divert attention from traffic
    • Class 3: Highest visibility; high-speed traffic, poor conditions; full body coverage

    Other Body Protection

    • FR clothing: Flame-resistant for flash fire/arc flash exposure (NFPA 2112/70E)
    • Chemical suits: Level A through D depending on hazard (HAZWOPER)
    • Aprons/chaps: Chain saw chaps (ASTM F1897), welding aprons/leathers
    Fall Protection Equipment — ANSI Z359 ▶
    Personal Fall Protection Equipment

    Components

    • Full-body harness: Required for fall arrest (body belts prohibited for fall arrest since 1998)
    • Lanyard: Shock-absorbing, max 6 feet; limits arresting force to under 1,800 lbs (900 lbs if free fall over 6 feet with deceleration device)
    • Self-retracting lifeline (SRL): Limits free fall to 2 feet; allows greater mobility
    • Anchorage: 5,000 lbs per employee, or designed by a qualified person with a 2:1 safety factor
    • Horizontal lifeline: Must be designed by a qualified person

    Inspection Before Each Use

    • Harness: check webbing for cuts, burns, fraying, chemical damage; test buckles and D-rings
    • Lanyard: inspect for damage, check snap hooks for gate function
    • SRL: extend and retract, listen for smooth operation, check housing for cracks
    • After a fall: harness, lanyard, and all components must be removed from service immediately

    Fall Clearance Calculation

    • Total fall distance = free fall + deceleration distance + harness stretch + height of worker + safety factor
    • Example: 6 ft lanyard + 3.5 ft deceleration + 1 ft D-ring shift + 6 ft worker height + 3 ft safety = 19.5 ft minimum clearance needed

    Chemical Hazards ▶
    Chemical Hazards

    Identification

    • Gases, vapors, mists, fumes, dusts, fibers, and liquids
    • Routes of exposure: inhalation, ingestion, absorption, injection
    • Common workplace chemicals: solvents, fuels, paints, adhesives, cleaning agents, welding fumes, silica dust, asbestos

    Controls

    • Elimination: Remove the chemical process entirely
    • Substitution: Replace with a less hazardous chemical (e.g., water-based vs solvent-based paint)
    • Engineering: Local exhaust ventilation, enclosed processes, wet methods for dust
    • Administrative: Limit exposure time, rotate workers, training, SDS availability
    • PPE: Respirators, chemical gloves, goggles, protective clothing

    Key Standards

    • HazCom/GHS: 1910.1200
    • PELs: 1910.1000 Tables Z-1, Z-2, Z-3
    • Substance-specific: asbestos (1910.1001), lead (1910.1025), benzene (1910.1028), silica (1910.1053)
    Biological Hazards ▶
    Biological Hazards

    Identification

    • Bacteria, viruses, fungi, mold, animal/insect bites, bloodborne pathogens
    • Industries: healthcare, labs, wastewater, agriculture, construction (soil/mold)

    Controls

    • Engineering: Biological safety cabinets, needle disposal containers, ventilation
    • Administrative: Exposure control plan, vaccination programs, hygiene practices, training
    • PPE: Gloves, face shields, gowns, respirators (N95+)

    Key Standards

    • Bloodborne Pathogens: 1910.1030
    • General Duty Clause: Section 5(a)(1) of the OSH Act
    Physical Hazards ▶
    Physical Hazards

    Identification

    • Noise, vibration, radiation (ionizing and non-ionizing), extreme temperatures, pressure extremes
    • Electrical hazards, falls, struck-by/caught-in/caught-between (the "Fatal Four")

    The Construction "Fatal Four" (leading causes of death)

    • Falls: 33.5% of construction fatalities
    • Struck-by: 11.1% of construction fatalities
    • Electrocution: 8.5% of construction fatalities
    • Caught-in/between: 5.5% of construction fatalities

    Controls

    • Engineering: Machine guarding, insulation, shielding, ventilation, fall protection systems
    • Administrative: Work/rest cycles (heat), rotation, safe work procedures, signage
    • PPE: Hearing protection, insulated gloves, FR clothing, fall arrest systems
    Ergonomic Hazards ▶
    Ergonomic Hazards

    Identification

    • Repetitive motions, forceful exertions, awkward postures, vibration, contact stress
    • Musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs): carpal tunnel, tendinitis, back injuries, rotator cuff
    • Risk factors: force, repetition, posture, duration, cold temperatures, vibration

    Controls

    • Engineering: Adjustable workstations, mechanical lifting aids, anti-vibration tools, ergonomic hand tools
    • Administrative: Job rotation, micro-breaks, stretching programs, training on proper lifting (keep load close, lift with legs)
    • PPE: Anti-vibration gloves, knee pads, back support (limited effectiveness)

    Key Standard

    • No specific OSHA ergonomics standard (the 2000 standard was repealed in 2001), but OSHA uses the General Duty Clause for severe ergonomic hazards
    • OSHA Ergonomics Guidelines available for specific industries
    Psychosocial Hazards ▶
    Psychosocial Hazards

    Identification

    • Workplace violence, harassment, excessive workload, shift work, job insecurity
    • Fatigue, stress, burnout, substance abuse
    • High-risk industries: healthcare, retail, transportation, construction, emergency services

    Controls

    • Engineering: Security systems, adequate lighting, barriers, alarm systems
    • Administrative: Workplace violence prevention programs, Employee Assistance Programs (EAP), reasonable work schedules, anti-harassment policies, mental health resources
    • PPE: Limited application; body cameras in some settings

    Key Standard

    • General Duty Clause: Section 5(a)(1) for workplace violence hazards
    • OSHA has issued guidance on preventing workplace violence in several industries

    Who Must Keep Records

    • Employers with 11 or more employees (at any time in the previous year)

    Toolbox Talks — 5-Minute Safety Meetings

    Pre-written topics for daily or weekly safety meetings. Click a topic to expand the full talk. Each is designed to be delivered in approximately 5 minutes.

    Heat Stress Prevention
    ~5 minutes | Best for: Summer months, outdoor crews

    Key Points

    • Heat-related illness kills dozens of workers every year and sends thousands to the ER
    • Three conditions: heat cramps (muscle pain, sweating), heat exhaustion (heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, cool/pale skin), heat stroke (hot/red/dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness — CALL 911)
    • Prevention: Water, Rest, Shade — OSHA's three-word campaign
    • Drink water every 15 minutes — do not wait until you are thirsty
    • New workers: acclimatize over 7-14 days by gradually increasing workload/exposure
    • Use the buddy system — watch for symptoms in coworkers
    • Know the signs: confusion, slurred speech, fainting, seizures = heat stroke = 911 immediately

    Discussion Question

    What is our plan today if someone shows signs of heat exhaustion? Who calls for help and where is the nearest shade/cooling area?

    Ladder Safety
    ~5 minutes | Best for: All trades

    Key Points

    • Falls from ladders cause approximately 20,000 injuries per year and are a top OSHA citation
    • 4:1 rule: set the base 1 foot away from the wall for every 4 feet of height
    • 3-foot rule: extend the ladder at least 3 feet above the landing surface
    • Three points of contact at all times (two hands + one foot, or two feet + one hand)
    • Never stand on the top two rungs of a stepladder or top three rungs of an extension ladder
    • Face the ladder when climbing; carry tools in a belt or haul them up with a rope
    • Inspect before each use: cracked rails, missing rungs, broken feet, damaged locks
    • Do not use metal ladders near electrical hazards

    Discussion Question

    Has anyone seen or experienced a ladder incident? What could have prevented it?

    Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
    ~5 minutes | Best for: Maintenance, electrical, mechanical crews

    Key Points

    • Failure to control hazardous energy causes roughly 50,000 injuries and 120 deaths per year
    • Six-step LOTO process:
      1. Prepare for shutdown — identify all energy sources
      2. Shut down the equipment
      3. Isolate energy sources (disconnect, close valves, block)
      4. Apply your personal lock and tag
      5. Release/restrain stored energy (bleed, block, drain, ground)
      6. Verify zero energy — try to start the machine
    • One lock per person — nobody removes your lock but YOU
    • Tags are NOT locks — use them only when a lock cannot be applied
    • Remember all energy types: electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, thermal, chemical, gravitational, mechanical, stored spring

    Discussion Question

    Where on this job site do we have equipment requiring LOTO? Does everyone know where the machine-specific procedures are posted?

    Trenching & Excavation Safety
    ~5 minutes | Best for: Utility, earthwork, pipe crews

    Key Points

    • One cubic yard of soil weighs approximately 3,000 pounds — a trench collapse can crush and suffocate a worker in seconds
    • Protective system required at 5 feet of depth (unless excavation is entirely in stable rock)
    • Options: slope, bench, shore, or shield based on soil classification
    • Call 811 at least 2-3 business days before any digging to locate underground utilities
    • Competent person must inspect the trench daily and after rain, freezing, or vibration events
    • Means of egress (ladder, ramp, stairs) within 25 feet of lateral travel
    • Keep spoil piles at least 2 feet from the edge of the trench
    • Never enter an unprotected trench — even for "just a second"

    Discussion Question

    Who is the competent person for excavation on this project? What type of protective system are we using today and why?

    Electrical Safety Awareness
    ~5 minutes | Best for: All workers, especially non-electrical trades

    Key Points

    • Electrocution is one of the "Fatal Four" leading causes of death in construction
    • As little as 50 milliamps (0.05 amps) can cause cardiac arrest — a standard outlet delivers 15-20 amps
    • Overhead power line safety: stay at least 10 feet away from lines up to 50kV
    • GFCI protection: required on all construction-site receptacles — test your GFCI before each use
    • Extension cords: inspect daily, no repairs with electrical tape, three-prong only (never remove the ground pin)
    • Wet conditions increase the risk — keep electrical connections off the ground and dry
    • If someone is being electrocuted: do NOT touch them. Disconnect power source first, then call 911

    Discussion Question

    Where are the GFCI devices on our site? When was the last time you tested yours?

    PPE — Why It Matters
    ~5 minutes | Best for: All workers, new employee orientation

    Key Points

    • PPE is the last line of defense — it does not eliminate the hazard, it protects YOU from the hazard
    • The employer pays for required PPE (except certain safety-toe footwear and prescription safety eyewear)
    • PPE must be selected based on the specific hazard — check the hazard assessment for your work area
    • Inspect PPE before every use: look for cracks, tears, wear, expired cartridges, missing parts
    • Wearing PPE incorrectly is as dangerous as not wearing it at all (e.g., safety glasses on your forehead do not protect your eyes)
    • Report damaged PPE immediately and get a replacement — never use defective PPE
    • Store PPE properly: clean, dry, away from sunlight and chemicals

    Discussion Question

    Is there any task today where you are unsure about the right PPE? Let us figure it out now before the work starts.

    Housekeeping on the Jobsite
    ~5 minutes | Best for: All crews, especially end of shift

    Key Points

    • Poor housekeeping is a leading cause of slips, trips, and falls — the most common type of workplace injury
    • Clean as you go: do not let debris, scrap, and trash accumulate throughout the day
    • Cords, hoses, and welding leads: route them so they do not cross walkways; use cord covers if unavoidable
    • Tools and materials: store them properly when not in use; do not leave them on the floor or in walkways
    • Spills: clean up immediately; if oil or grease, use absorbent material and mark the area
    • Trash containers: empty them regularly; do not let them overflow
    • Good housekeeping also reduces fire risk by eliminating fuel sources for accidental ignition

    Discussion Question

    Walk the area right now: does anyone see a housekeeping hazard we should address before work begins?

    Fall Protection
    ~5 minutes | Best for: Any elevated work

    Key Points

    • Falls are the #1 cause of death in construction — over 300 construction workers die from falls each year
    • Fall protection required at 6 feet in construction, 4 feet in general industry
    • Three systems: guardrails, safety nets, personal fall arrest (harness + lanyard + anchorage)
    • Harness inspection before every use: webbing, D-rings, buckles, stitching, labels
    • After a fall, the harness, lanyard, and all components MUST be taken out of service
    • Fall clearance: calculate the total distance needed below your anchorage — 6-ft lanyard does NOT mean you are safe at 6 feet above ground
    • 100% tie-off: maintain connection at all times when above the trigger height
    • Floor holes and openings: cover them immediately and mark "HOLE" on the cover

    Discussion Question

    Point out every fall hazard in your work area today. What fall protection system are we using and is it adequate?

    Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Steps ▶
    LOTO Procedure — Six Steps

    Step 1: PREPARE

    Identify all energy sources (electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, thermal, chemical, gravitational, mechanical, stored). Review machine-specific LOTO procedure. Notify all affected employees.

    Step 2: SHUT DOWN

    Turn off the machine using the normal stopping procedure. Do not just pull the plug or hit the E-stop.

    Step 3: ISOLATE

    Disconnect from all energy sources: open breakers, close valves, disconnect hydraulic lines, block mechanical energy.

    Step 4: LOCK & TAG

    Apply YOUR personal lock and tag to each energy isolation device. Each authorized employee applies their own lock. Use a multi-lock hasp for group lockout.

    Step 5: RELEASE STORED ENERGY

    Bleed hydraulic/pneumatic lines. Discharge capacitors. Block raised components. Release or restrain springs. Allow hot surfaces to cool. Ground residual electrical energy.

    Step 6: VERIFY

    Attempt to start the machine using normal controls. Check voltage/pressure/flow gauges at zero. Confirm zero energy state. ONLY THEN begin work.

    Fire Extinguisher Types & PASS Method ▶
    Fire Extinguisher Selection & Use

    Fire Classes & Extinguisher Types

    ClassFuel TypeExtinguisher Agent
    AOrdinary combustibles (wood, paper, cloth)Water, foam, dry chemical, wet chemical
    BFlammable liquids (gas, oil, grease, solvents)CO2, dry chemical, foam, clean agent
    CEnergized electrical equipmentCO2, dry chemical, clean agent (NEVER water)
    DCombustible metals (magnesium, titanium, sodium)Special dry powder (specific to the metal)
    KCooking oils and fats (commercial kitchens)Wet chemical

    PASS Method

    P — Pull the pin. Hold the extinguisher with the nozzle pointing away from you.

    A — Aim low. Point the nozzle at the BASE of the fire.

    S — Squeeze the handle slowly and evenly.

    S — Sweep the nozzle from side to side at the base of the fire until it goes out.

    Remember: only attempt to fight a fire if you are trained, the fire is small, you have a clear escape route, and the correct extinguisher is available. When in doubt, GET OUT and call 911.
