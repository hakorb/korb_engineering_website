Who this is for

People fleeing persecution or danger in their home country who are seeking safety in the United States. This includes refugees (applying from outside the U.S.), asylum seekers (already in the U.S. or at a port of entry), and people exploring related protections like Temporary Protected Status (TPS), Withholding of Removal, or relief under the Convention Against Torture (CAT).

Refugee vs. Asylee — the key difference

Both protect people fleeing persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. The practical difference:

  • Refugee — you apply from outside the United States, usually through UNHCR and a U.S. Refugee Admissions Program (USRAP) resettlement agency. You are vetted overseas and travel to the U.S. already with refugee status.
  • Asylum seeker / Asylee — you are already in the U.S. (or arriving at a border/port of entry) and apply for protection from here. You must file within one year of arrival unless you qualify for an exception.

Other related protections

  • TPS (Temporary Protected Status) — for nationals of designated countries facing armed conflict, disaster, or extraordinary conditions. Time-limited; must re-register each period.
  • Withholding of Removal — like asylum but higher standard of proof ("more likely than not"). Does not lead to permanent residency or family petitions, but prevents deportation to the country of persecution.
  • CAT protection — for people who would more likely than not be tortured if removed. Does not require a protected ground.
  • Special Immigrant Juvenile (SIJ) — for minors abandoned or abused.
  • VAWA / U-visa / T-visa — for victims of domestic violence (VAWA), crime (U), or trafficking (T).
  • Humanitarian parole — temporary permission to enter for urgent humanitarian reasons.

Three things every asylum seeker should know today

  1. The one-year deadline. Asylum must generally be filed within one year of arrival in the U.S. Missing this without a valid exception (changed circumstances, extraordinary circumstances) usually blocks asylum permanently — but you may still qualify for Withholding or CAT.
  2. Document your story. Write down dates, names, places of persecution incidents while memory is fresh. Keep any evidence — threatening letters, medical records, news articles, country-condition reports.
  3. Do not leave the U.S. Traveling back to your country of feared persecution can destroy your asylum claim. Even travel to other countries can create issues — consult an attorney first.
Scam warning. "Notarios," "consultants," and unlicensed "immigration advisors" who charge for services are illegal in most states and have ruined many cases. Only a licensed attorney or BIA-accredited representative can legally represent you in immigration matters. Free, qualified help exists — see LEGAL AID tab.
AFFIRMATIVE ASYLUM PROCESS

Filing with USCIS when you are not in removal proceedings.

Step 1 — File Form I-589

File the Application for Asylum and for Withholding of Removal within one year of your most recent arrival. The form is free to file. Include personal statement + evidence.

Step 2 — Biometrics

USCIS sends a notice for fingerprints at an Application Support Center.

Step 3 — Work authorization

Once your I-589 has been pending 150 days (the "asylum clock"), you may file Form I-765 (category c(8)) for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD). There is no fee for initial c(8) EAD.

Step 4 — Asylum interview

You will be scheduled for a non-adversarial interview at an Asylum Office. Bring interpreter, documents, and if possible a lawyer or accredited rep.

Step 5 — Decision

Granted (you become an asylee), referred to immigration court (removal proceedings), or NOID (Notice of Intent to Deny).

Step 6 — After asylum granted

After 1 year as an asylee, apply for a green card (Form I-485). Family reunification via Form I-730 is available for spouse and unmarried children under 21 within 2 years of grant.

DEFENSIVE ASYLUM (IN IMMIGRATION COURT)

When you are already in removal proceedings (before an Immigration Judge).

Step 1 — Master Calendar Hearing

The judge asks you to plead to the charges and identify what relief you are seeking (asylum, withholding, CAT, cancellation of removal, voluntary departure, etc.).

Step 2 — File Form I-589 with the court

If seeking asylum, submit the I-589 directly to the immigration court (not USCIS).

Step 3 — Individual Hearing

This is your trial. You testify, government attorney may cross-examine, witnesses may be called, evidence is submitted.

Step 4 — Judge's decision

Granted, denied, or voluntary departure. Denials can be appealed to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) within 30 days.

In court without an attorney? You have the right to representation but NOT at government expense. Start calling the legal-aid directory before your next hearing date.
CREDIBLE FEAR / REASONABLE FEAR INTERVIEWS

If you are detained after entering the U.S. and express fear of return, you will be interviewed by an Asylum Officer to determine "credible fear" (for most people) or "reasonable fear" (for those with prior removal orders or certain criminal history).

  • Tell the officer everything — all reasons you are afraid, who threatened you, when, how.
  • Request an interpreter in your best language. Do not agree to English if you are not fluent.
  • If you don't pass, you can request a review by an Immigration Judge (usually within 7 days).
  • Free legal help is available in some detention facilities via the Legal Orientation Program — ask the guard.

YOUR RIGHTS IN THE UNITED STATES

Constitutional rights apply to everyone on U.S. soil

Regardless of immigration status, you have basic constitutional rights, including:

  • Right to remain silent — you do not have to answer questions about your immigration status. Ask: "Am I free to go?"
  • Right to refuse a warrantless search of your home or belongings. Ask to see a judicial warrant signed by a judge.
  • Right to an attorney in immigration court — but you must find and pay for one (except in specific programs). Do not sign anything you do not understand.
  • Right to make a phone call if arrested.
  • Right to an interpreter in your best language at an immigration interview or hearing.

If ICE comes to your home

  • Ask if they have a judicial warrant (signed by a judge, not ICE). Only a judicial warrant allows them to enter without your consent.
  • Keep the door closed. Speak through the door. Ask them to slide the warrant under.
  • Do not sign anything. Do not lie. Do not show false documents.
  • Tell them: "I wish to remain silent. I want to speak with an attorney."

If you are detained

  • You have the right to a list of free legal services — ask the detention facility for it.
  • You may be eligible for a bond hearing before an Immigration Judge (in most cases).
  • Memorize A-numbers and contact information for family — phone access is limited.
  • Do not sign a voluntary departure or stipulated removal order without legal advice — it can waive your right to asylum.

Children's rights

  • Unaccompanied minors (under 18, no parent in U.S.) are referred to ORR (Office of Refugee Resettlement) within 72 hours of apprehension.
  • Minors in removal proceedings have the right to seek counsel — many nonprofits specialize in pro bono representation.
  • SIJ status may be available for children abused, abandoned, or neglected by one or both parents.

GLOSSARY OF COMMON TERMS

A-number — Alien Registration Number, a unique 8-9 digit case identifier assigned by USCIS/DHS. Starts with "A".

Asylee — A person granted asylum in the U.S.

BIA — Board of Immigration Appeals. Handles appeals from immigration judge decisions.

BIA-Accredited Representative — A non-lawyer authorized by the DOJ to represent immigrants; must work with a recognized nonprofit.

CAT — Convention Against Torture protection.

Credible Fear Interview (CFI) — The initial screening for detained arrivals expressing fear of return.

DHS — Department of Homeland Security (parent of USCIS, ICE, CBP).

EAD — Employment Authorization Document (work permit).

EOIR — Executive Office for Immigration Review (the immigration court system, part of DOJ).

Form I-589 — Application for Asylum and for Withholding of Removal.

Form I-765 — Application for Employment Authorization.

Form I-730 — Refugee/Asylee Relative Petition (for spouse & unmarried children under 21).

Form I-485 — Application to Register Permanent Residence (green card application).

ICE — Immigration and Customs Enforcement (enforcement/detention/removal).

Master Calendar Hearing — Short preliminary hearing in immigration court.

Notice to Appear (NTA) — The document that starts removal proceedings.

One-year bar — The deadline to file affirmative asylum within one year of arrival.

ORR — Office of Refugee Resettlement (HHS), cares for unaccompanied minors and refugee resettlement.

Parole — Temporary permission to enter the U.S. for urgent humanitarian reasons.

Pro bono — Free legal services provided by an attorney.

PSG — Particular Social Group (one of the protected grounds for asylum).

Refugee — A person granted refugee status while overseas, resettled to the U.S.

Removal / deportation — The legal process of ordering someone to leave the U.S.

SIJ — Special Immigrant Juvenile status.

TPS — Temporary Protected Status.

USCIS — U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (adjudicates benefit applications).

USRAP — U.S. Refugee Admissions Program.

Withholding of Removal — Prevents deportation to the country of feared persecution; higher standard than asylum.
