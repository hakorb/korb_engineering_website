🏆 Golden Rules of Scope Management
Document everything in writing
Never start additional work without written authorization
Issue timely notice per contract requirements
Track costs separately for each change
Maintain the relationship — it's a marathon, not a sprint
Present solutions, not just problems
Quantify impacts in dollars and days
Reference the contract, not emotions
Consider the other party's perspective
When in doubt, over-communicate
📖 Common Contract Clause Explanations

Changes Clause

Provides the owner the right to make changes to the scope of work during construction. Typically requires written authorization and establishes the mechanism for adjusting the contract price and time. Without a changes clause, any modification could be considered a breach of contract. Key point: Follow the prescribed change order process exactly.

Differing Site Conditions (DSC)

Protects the contractor when subsurface or latent physical conditions differ materially from those indicated in the contract documents (Type I) or from those ordinarily encountered (Type II). The contractor must give prompt written notice. This clause allocates the risk of the unknown to the party best able to investigate — typically the owner.

Force Majeure

Excuses performance when extraordinary events beyond either party's control occur (natural disasters, pandemics, war, government actions). Typically provides time relief but not additional compensation. Key: Notice requirements are usually strict — notify immediately and document the impact.

No Damages for Delay

Limits the contractor's remedy for owner-caused delays to time extensions only (no additional compensation). These clauses are common but have exceptions in many jurisdictions: active interference, bad faith, delays beyond contemplation, and abandonment. Courts increasingly scrutinize these clauses.

Pay-if-Paid vs. Pay-when-Paid

Pay-if-Paid: A condition precedent — the contractor's obligation to pay the subcontractor arises only if the owner pays the contractor. Shifts the risk of owner non-payment to the subcontractor.

Pay-when-Paid: Merely a timing mechanism establishing a reasonable time for payment. The contractor remains obligated to pay regardless of whether they receive payment from the owner. Courts in many states presume pay-when-paid unless pay-if-paid language is explicit and unambiguous.

Liquidated Damages

Pre-agreed daily or milestone-based damages for late completion. Must be a reasonable estimate of actual damages at the time of contracting — if grossly disproportionate, courts may find them to be an unenforceable penalty. Contractors should factor this risk into their bids and diligently document any owner-caused delays.

Limitation of Liability

Caps one or both parties' total liability, often excluding consequential, special, or incidental damages. Common in professional services agreements. Important: Ensure the cap is proportional to the contract value and your risk exposure.

Dispute Resolution

Establishes the method for resolving disputes: negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or litigation. Many contracts require a stepped process (negotiate first, then mediate, then arbitrate/litigate). Key: Know your contract's dispute resolution ladder and follow it — failure to do so may waive your rights.

📚 Industry Standard References

AIA A201 — General Conditions

The most widely used general conditions document in the U.S. construction industry. Published by the American Institute of Architects. Establishes the rights, responsibilities, and relationships of the owner, contractor, and architect. Article 7 covers changes in the work; Article 15 covers claims and disputes.

EJCDC — Engineers Joint Contract Documents Committee

Standard documents commonly used on civil/infrastructure projects, particularly those designed by engineers rather than architects. More balanced allocation of risk than some other forms. The C-700 series covers standard general conditions for construction.

ConsensusDocs

Developed collaboratively by 40+ construction associations representing owners, contractors, subcontractors, and designers. Intended to be more balanced and fair than documents drafted primarily by one stakeholder group. ConsensusDocs 200 is the standard agreement and general conditions.
