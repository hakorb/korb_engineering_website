COMMON EXECUTIVE QUESTIONS BY CATEGORY

Budget Requests

"What's the ROI and payback period?"
Always lead with hard numbers. If the payback is over 3 years, you need a very strong strategic rationale.

"Can we do this for half the cost?"
Prepare a tiered approach: minimum viable, recommended, and premium options with clear tradeoffs.

"What happens if we delay this a year?"
Quantify the cost of inaction. Lost revenue, compliance risk, competitive disadvantage.

New Projects

"Why this project over other priorities?"
Frame against strategic pillars. Show alignment with published company objectives.

"What's the opportunity cost?"
Acknowledge what you will NOT do. Executives respect honesty about tradeoffs.

"Who else is doing this?"
Industry benchmarks and competitor analysis. If nobody else is doing it, explain why you should be first.

Process Changes

"What's wrong with the current process?"
Use metrics: current cost, error rates, time spent, customer complaints. Never just say "it's inefficient."

"How much disruption will this cause?"
Provide a transition plan with milestones. Acknowledge the dip in productivity during changeover.

Staffing

"Can't we automate this instead?"
Show the human judgment gap. What decisions require expertise that cannot be automated?

"What if we use contractors?"
Compare total cost: FTE loaded cost vs. contractor rates. Include knowledge retention risk.

Technology

"What's the implementation risk?"
Reference similar deployments. Cite vendor track record. Have a rollback plan.

"Why this vendor over alternatives?"
Decision matrix: features, cost, support, integration, references. At least 3 alternatives evaluated.

POWER ANSWER FRAMEWORK: SIAR

S
Situation - Set the context. What is the current state? Keep it to 1-2 sentences.
I
Impact - Why does this matter? Quantify with dollars, time, risk, or strategic value.
A
Action - What specifically are you proposing? Be precise on scope, timeline, resources.
R
Result - What outcome will this deliver? Tie back to metrics and strategic goals.

HANDLING "I DON'T KNOW"

Never bluff. Executives detect BS instantly. Instead:

1. Acknowledge honestly: "I don't have that specific data point right now."

2. Offer a timeline: "I can have that analysis to you by end of day Thursday."

3. Bridge to what you know: "What I can tell you is [related data point that supports your case]."

4. Reframe if possible: "That's a great question. The way we approached this was [different angle]."

Never say: "I'm not sure," "I think maybe," or "That's a good question" (without following up with substance).

ETHICAL DEFLECTION TECHNIQUES

Redirect to strengths:
"While I see that concern, the more critical factor here is [your strongest point]."

Scope narrowing:
"That's a broader strategic question. For this specific proposal, the relevant consideration is..."

Parking lot:
"Excellent point. Let's table that for the deeper dive and focus on the decision framework today."

Preemptive concession:
"You're right that [minor weakness]. That's exactly why we built in [mitigation]."

Agree and pivot:
"I share that concern. Here's how we've addressed it in the plan..."

DATA PRESENTATION TIPS

Rule of 3: No more than 3 key numbers per slide. Executives remember 3 things.

Anchor high: Show the cost of NOT acting before showing your budget ask.

Relative framing: "$1.2M" means nothing. "$1.2M, or 0.3% of annual revenue" means everything.

Time horizon: Always show cumulative 3-year or 5-year impact, not just year 1.

Visual proof: One chart beats ten paragraphs. Before/after comparisons are powerful.

Source everything: "Industry reports suggest..." is weak. "Gartner 2025 Q3 report shows..." is strong.
