Key Principles

BATNA

Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. Your power comes from your ability to walk away. Never enter a negotiation without knowing your BATNA. The stronger your alternative, the more leverage you have.

ZOPA

Zone Of Possible Agreement. The overlap between your walk-away point and theirs. If no ZOPA exists, no deal is possible. Your job is to discover the ZOPA and claim value within it.

Anchoring

The first number on the table disproportionately influences the outcome. Make an ambitious but justifiable first offer. Research shows anchoring effects persist even when people know about them.

Framing

How you present information matters as much as the information itself. Frame proposals in terms of the other party's interests. "This protects your timeline" beats "We need more money."

Harvard Method

Separate people from problems. Focus on interests, not positions. Invent options for mutual gain. Use objective criteria. The goal is a wise agreement that meets legitimate interests of both sides.

Information Asymmetry

The party with more information has more power. Ask open-ended questions. Listen more than you talk. Every piece of information you learn about their constraints, deadlines, and alternatives strengthens your position.

Tactics Library

Mirroring

Repeat the last 1-3 words of what they said as a question. This encourages them to elaborate and reveal information. Remarkably effective and simple. "You're saying the budget is fixed?"

Labeling Emotions

"It seems like you're concerned about..." Naming their emotion defuses it and shows empathy. People become more cooperative when they feel understood, even in adversarial negotiations.

Calibrated Questions

Ask "How am I supposed to do that?" or "What would you do in my position?" These force the other side to solve your problem. They feel in control while actually working for you.

Strategic Silence

After making an offer or hearing one, pause. Silence creates pressure. Most people rush to fill it with concessions. Count to ten in your head.

Package Dealing

Never negotiate single issues. Bundle terms together. "I can do X on price if you can do Y on timeline." This expands the pie and creates trades where both sides gain.

Nibbling

After reaching agreement on major terms, ask for small additional concessions. "Great, and you'll cover shipping, right?" Works because people don't want to reopen a done deal.

Flinch

React visibly (or verbally) to their first offer. "Wow, that's way outside our range." Even if the offer is reasonable, a flinch signals they need to move. Never accept a first offer.

Bracketing

If your target is $50K, and they offer $40K, counter at $60K. The midpoint ($50K) is your target. Start at a point where splitting the difference gives you what you want.

Time Pressure

Create or leverage deadlines. "I have another offer that expires Friday." But beware: if they detect a false deadline, you lose credibility. Use real constraints when possible.

Good Cop / Bad Cop

"I'd love to agree, but my partner/boss would never approve that." Creates an invisible third party who constrains your flexibility. Useful for setting limits without being the villain.

Common Mistakes

Conceding Too Fast

Each concession should be smaller than the last and harder to get. If you make large, quick concessions, they'll expect more. Make them work for every inch.

Revealing Your BATNA Too Early

If your BATNA is weak, never share it. If strong, hint at it without specifics. "We have other options we're evaluating" is better than giving details they can undermine.

Getting Emotional

Anger, frustration, and ego cloud judgment. Take a break if emotions run high. The best negotiators are calm under pressure. Let the other side get emotional -- it gives you information.

Negotiating Against Yourself

Never improve your offer without getting something in return. If they reject your proposal, don't immediately offer better terms. Ask what they'd propose instead.

Focusing on Position, Not Interests

"I want $50K" is a position. "I need to cover equipment costs and 15% margin" is an interest. Understanding why unlocks creative solutions.

Power Moves by Scenario

Salary / Raise

Anchor with market data. Quantify your contributions in dollars. Have a competing offer if possible. Negotiate total comp, not just base. Ask: "What would it take for me to earn X here?"

Construction Change Order

Document everything before the conversation. Show cost breakdowns with backup. Reference contract terms. Frame as risk mitigation for the owner. Time is your friend -- delay costs them more.

Contract / Vendor

Get multiple bids. Use volume as leverage. Negotiate payment terms separately from price. Ask for performance guarantees. Long-term relationships can justify short-term concessions.

Real Estate

Know comps cold. Inspect thoroughly and use findings as leverage. The motivated party loses. Control the timeline. Escalation clauses and contingencies are powerful tools.
