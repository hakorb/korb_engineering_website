Gmail: Open the email → Click three dots (top-right) → "Show original" → Copy headers

Outlook (Web): Open the email → Click three dots → "View message details" or "View message source"

Outlook (Desktop): Double-click to open → File → Properties → "Internet headers" box

Apple Mail: Open the email → View menu → Message → All Headers (or Cmd+Shift+H)

Yahoo: Open the email → Three dots → "View raw message"

Immediate Steps When You Receive a Threat

DO NOT respond to the threat. Any engagement can escalate the situation and may compromise your legal position.
  1. Screenshot and preserve ALL evidence immediately. Capture the full message, profile, timestamp, and any attachments before the sender can delete them.
  2. Save full email headers before anything else. Headers contain routing data that can identify the sender. Use the Headers tab in this tool to analyze them.
  3. Document the time, date, and context. Note what you were doing, any preceding interactions, and why you believe the message is threatening. Use the Threat Log tab.
  4. Do not forward the original email to anyone except law enforcement or your attorney -- forwarding can alter headers.
  5. Save originals in a dedicated folder on your computer. Back up to a second location (cloud storage or external drive).

File a Police Report

Contact your local police department's non-emergency line. Bring printed copies of all evidence. Template language:

"I am reporting threatening/harassing communications received via [platform]. I have preserved the original messages including full email headers and metadata. The communications began on [date] and include [brief description]. I am requesting a police report number for documentation purposes and to support potential further legal action."

Request a copy of the police report and the report number for your records.

Federal Reporting

  • FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3): ic3.gov -- File a complaint for cyber threats, interstate threats, or threats involving electronic communications.
  • CISA: cisa.gov/report -- Report cybersecurity incidents.

Contact your ISP or email provider's abuse department for additional header information and to request they preserve server logs.

Legal Reference

  • 18 U.S.C. § 875 -- Interstate communications: criminalizes threats transmitted in interstate or foreign commerce (including email, social media).
  • 18 U.S.C. § 2261A -- Federal cyberstalking statute: criminalizes using electronic communications to harass, intimidate, or cause substantial emotional distress.
  • State laws vary. Most states have specific cyberstalking, harassment, and threat statutes. Check your state attorney general's website.

Consider a restraining order if the identity of the sender is known. Many jurisdictions allow restraining orders based on electronic harassment.

When to Involve an Attorney

  • If threats are persistent or escalating
  • If the sender's identity is known and you want to pursue civil action
  • If threats involve your workplace, clients, or professional reputation
  • If law enforcement is unresponsive or you need help navigating the legal system
  • If you receive threats from someone in another state or country

If work-related, inform your employer's security team immediately.
