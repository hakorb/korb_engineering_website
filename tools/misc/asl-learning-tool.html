About ASL

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language with its own grammar and syntax. It is not simply signed English. ASL has its own word order, idioms, and linguistic rules that differ significantly from spoken English.

ASL is the primary language of many Deaf and hard-of-hearing people in North America. It is a rich, expressive language with regional dialects and a vibrant cultural community.

Key things to know:

  • ASL uses handshapes, facial expressions, body movement, and spatial relationships to convey meaning.
  • Fingerspelling (the manual alphabet) is used for proper nouns and words without established signs.
  • This tool teaches the ASL manual alphabet and common signs — it is a starting point, not a complete course.
  • To truly learn ASL, seek out classes taught by Deaf instructors and engage with the Deaf community.
  • Organizations like the National Association of the Deaf (NAD) and local Deaf cultural centers are great resources.

This tool is for educational purposes. Always approach ASL and Deaf culture with respect and a willingness to learn.
