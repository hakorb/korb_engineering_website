Reference — Know Your Rights FOIA & public records primer

What records can you request?

Generally: any record (paper, email, database export, video, audio, report, contract, memo, calendar, text message) created, received, or maintained by the agency in the course of government business. Personal notes, drafts-in-progress, and records not yet received by the agency may be excluded.

Tips: Request records by subject + date range + custodian. Ask for electronic format to reduce duplication fees. Request metadata where relevant (email headers, edit history).

The 9 Federal FOIA Exemptions (5 USC 552(b))

ExemptionScope
b(1)Classified national defense / foreign policy info properly marked under Executive Order
b(2)Records related solely to internal personnel rules and practices
b(3)Information specifically exempted from disclosure by another statute
b(4)Trade secrets and confidential commercial / financial information obtained from a person
b(5)Inter-agency or intra-agency pre-decisional / deliberative memoranda (limited after FOIA Improvement Act 2016)
b(6)Personnel, medical, and similar files where disclosure constitutes clearly unwarranted invasion of personal privacy
b(7)Law enforcement records (6 sub-exemptions: ongoing proceedings, fair trial, privacy, sources, techniques, safety)
b(8)Records from examination, operation, or condition reports of financial institutions
b(9)Geological and geophysical information and data concerning wells

If you are denied

  • The agency MUST tell you which exemption applies and provide instructions for appeal.
  • Administrative appeal typically must be filed within 90 days of the denial (federal; state timelines vary).
  • Use the same request tracking number. Demand the agency release all reasonably segregable non-exempt portions.
  • If the administrative appeal fails, you may sue in U.S. District Court (or state court equivalent). FOIA litigation has attorney-fee shifting when you substantially prevail.
  • Contact the Office of Government Information Services (OGIS) for federal FOIA mediation: archives.gov/ogis

Pro tips

  • Be specific. "All records related to X" gets rejected as overbroad. Use date range + custodian + keyword.
  • Request electronic format. Cheaper, faster, searchable.
  • Don't pay unreasonable fees. You can challenge fee estimates. Commercial requesters pay more — ensure your category is correct.
  • If the agency misses the statutory deadline, you have "constructively exhausted" administrative remedies and may sue. Write a follow-up citing the missed deadline before escalating.
  • State laws differ. Some states require residency; most don't. Response windows range 3–60 days.
  • Save everything. Keep copies of your request, the agency's response, and all correspondence.

Drafting tips by request type

  • Email / Slack / Teams records: Name the custodian(s), specify the domain (@agency.gov), and request native format with headers + attachments. For Slack/Teams, request channel names or DM pairs.
  • Contracts & procurement: Request the solicitation, all bids, the awarded contract (with modifications), invoices, and correspondence with the vendor. Cite contract numbers if known.
  • Calendars & schedules: Specify the official and date range. Most agencies maintain calendar records in Outlook/Google; request native export.
  • Law enforcement: Expect heavy b(7) redactions. Request the case number. Consider requesting only "closed" cases to avoid the b(7)(A) ongoing-investigation exemption.
  • Body-worn camera & 911: State laws vary widely — some require a waiver or court order. Check your state's bodycam statute separately.
  • Data / databases: Many states require agencies to produce data in its native format (not scanned PDFs). Ask for CSV, JSON, or database export; cite the applicable statutory provision.
  • Text messages / personal accounts: Cite recent case law (e.g., federal Competitive Enterprise Institute v. OSTP) confirming that agency business on personal devices is still subject to FOIA.

Response workflow — what to expect

  1. Acknowledgement (1–20 days): Agency assigns a tracking number and categorizes your request.
  2. Fee estimate (if you didn't waive fees): Agency estimates search + review + duplication costs.
  3. Search & review (weeks to years): Records located, reviewed for exemptions, redacted.
  4. Determination: Full release / partial release / denial. Must cite exemption(s).
  5. Production: Records delivered via portal, email, mail, or in-person inspection.
  6. Administrative appeal (90 days federal): File with agency's appeals officer.
  7. Litigation (after appeal exhausted or deadline missed): U.S. District Court for federal; state court for state requests.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Asking for information, not records. FOIA produces existing records; it does not compel agencies to create new records or answer questions. Reframe "why did you do X?" as "all records documenting the decision to do X."
  • Overbreadth. Requests that would produce millions of pages get rejected or delayed. Narrow by time + custodian + keyword.
  • Underspecificity. "All records about immigration" → rejected. "All emails between Assistant Secretary Jones and any DHS employee, 1/1/2024–6/1/2024, containing the word 'parole'" → accepted.
  • Paying fees you shouldn't. If the agency misses its statutory deadline, it generally can't charge search fees (commercial requesters excepted). Cite 5 USC 552(a)(4)(A)(viii) at the federal level.
  • Forgetting to appeal. Administrative appeal is free and often the agency reverses. File even if you're uncertain — it's a prerequisite to suing.
  • Missing appeal deadlines. Federal: 90 days. State deadlines vary. Calendar the due date the moment you get a denial.

State law quirks worth knowing

  • Residency requirements: Alabama, Arkansas, Delaware, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Tennessee, and Virginia historically required requesters to be state residents (Virginia's requirement was upheld by SCOTUS in McBurney v. Young, 2013). Many agencies waive informally — if denied, ask a resident friend to file.
  • No deadline states: Alabama, Arizona, Florida, Minnesota, Montana, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Wisconsin — laws say "promptly" or "reasonable time" but set no numeric deadline. Courts enforce "reasonable" via suit.
  • Sunshine states: Florida has one of the strongest public records laws in the country; records are presumed open, anonymous requests are allowed, and courts must award fees if you prevail.
  • AG ruling states: Texas (uniquely) requires agencies to request an Attorney General opinion before withholding records. This creates a public paper trail of exemption claims.
  • Electronic preference: California, Colorado, Oregon, and Washington expressly require agencies to produce data in electronic/native format if the agency maintains it electronically.
  • Personnel records: Some states (California, Washington) are expanding police personnel/misconduct record access; others keep them sealed. Check SB 1421 (CA) and SB 12 (WA).
  • Time for review: Several states (CO, VA, MO) charge for time spent reviewing records for redaction. Request that agency itemize any review charges.

9 federal exemptions — with real-world examples

  • b(1) National security: CIA operational files, intelligence sources and methods, current classified briefings.
  • b(2) Internal rules: Agency-only HR procedures, guard-schedule procedures. Narrowed by Milner v. Dept. of Navy (2011) — no longer covers "high-2" law enforcement manuals.
  • b(3) Other statute: Tax returns (26 USC 6103), census responses (13 USC 9), grand jury material (Rule 6(e)).
  • b(4) Trade secrets: Pharmaceutical formulations in FDA submissions, bid protest confidentials. Food Marketing Institute v. Argus Leader (2019) expanded scope to any "customarily and actually treated as private" info.
  • b(5) Deliberative process: Draft memos, options papers, pre-decisional recommendations. FOIA Improvement Act 2016 added 25-year sunset — records older than 25 years cannot be withheld under b(5).
  • b(6) Personal privacy: Employee home addresses, medical records of federal workers. Balances privacy vs. public interest.
  • b(7)(A) Ongoing investigation: Most commonly used by FBI, DEA. Expires when case closes. (b)(7) sub-exemptions also protect sources, techniques, and witness safety.
  • b(8) Financial institution oversight: FDIC/Fed/OCC bank exam reports, CAMELS ratings.
  • b(9) Geological: Rarely invoked; protects oil and gas well data.

Sample appeal language

When you receive a denial, the appeal letter should (1) cite the tracking number, (2) identify each contested determination, (3) explain why each exemption is misapplied, and (4) demand segregability. Template opening:

"Pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 552(a)(6)(A)(i) and [agency] regulations at [cite], I hereby appeal the [date] determination regarding FOIA request [tracking #]. The agency withheld [X] records in full and redacted [Y] records under Exemption [N]. For the reasons that follow, the withholding is unjustified and must be reversed..."

Glossary

  • Custodian — the employee/office physically or electronically holding the records.
  • Glomar response — agency's refusal to confirm or deny the existence of records (common with FBI/CIA).
  • Vaughn index — in litigation, the itemized list agencies must produce describing each withheld document and exemption claimed.
  • Segregability — requirement that agencies release non-exempt portions of partially exempt records.
  • In camera review — a judge privately examining withheld records to assess exemption claims.
  • Mandatory declassification review (MDR) — separate process for declassifying classified records, sometimes faster than FOIA for b(1) material.
