I — The Visionary

Monte Walter Korb

Georgia Tech ’50 · PE · St. Simons Island, GA

Monte attended Georgia Institute of Technology under the GI Bill and received his Bachelor of Mechanical Engineering in 1950. He went on to work with Wernher von Braun at Redstone Arsenal in Huntsville, Alabama — where America’s reach for the stars was being forged in fire and steel.

In 1965 he received a patent for a nozzle attachment for the control of range and thrust of solid propellant rocket motors — technology that flew on the Saturn V. He became a licensed Professional Engineer in Georgia in 1968.

In 1972, Monte founded Korb Engineering Company on St. Simons Island, Georgia. He practiced engineering for over forty years before retiring in 2014. Where every lineage begins, there is someone who chose to build. Monte chose to build rockets — and then a family legacy.

II — The World Builder

Andrew Douglas Korb

Mechanical Engineer · FL PE License #26329
[ photo ]

Andy Korb took what his father built and carried it around the world. A mechanical engineer whose work knew no borders, he brought Korb precision to projects across the globe — proof that a foundation laid on a Georgia barrier island could reach anywhere.

He led Korb Engineering of Florida, Inc. for nearly three decades (1996–2025), building a practice that stood as the longest-running Korb firm in the state. His hands shaped more than material — they shaped a family’s understanding of what it means to show up, do the work right, and leave things better than you found them.

003
III — The Torchbearer

Monte Alan Korb

FL Engineering · Korb Engineering of Florida ’86
[ photo ]

Named for his father, Alan carried more than a name — he carried a calling. He brought the Korb legacy south, establishing Korb Engineering of Florida in 1986 and proving that what Monte built in Georgia could take root anywhere the work demanded.

Through McAllister-Gates and Korb Engineered Systems, he continued to evolve — adapting, building, solving. His career is the bridge between the Saturn V generation and the next, proof that the Korb instinct to engineer isn’t learned. It’s inherited.

He gave his son more than a last name. He gave him a blueprint for how to live: build things that matter, and never stop.
