Step 3 — Build Your Search Strategy

A good prior art search uses multiple angles: keywords, synonyms, classification codes, inventor names, and assignees. Here's what we auto-generated from your invention description.

Auto-Extracted Keywords (click to add to your search)
Pro tip — CPC vs IPC: CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification) is used by USPTO and EPO together and is more granular than IPC. Espacenet's "Classification search" finds related codes from a seed keyword. Once you have 2-3 CPC codes, run a classification search alongside a keyword search for maximum coverage.
Tip — Boolean operators: Most databases support AND, OR, NOT, and proximity operators (NEAR, ADJ). Use parentheses. Example: (impeller OR rotor) AND (centrifugal NEAR/3 pump) NOT axial
Step 4 — Search Prior Art Databases

Click a database card to open a pre-built query in a new tab. When you find something worth remembering, copy the patent number and click "Log reference" or jump to Step 5.

Search order we recommend: Google Patents first (broadest, includes non-English translations) → USPTO PPUBS for US-specific coverage → Espacenet for European + classification search → WIPO PatentScope for international applications → Google Scholar for non-patent literature (NPL) like conference papers and journals, which are also prior art under 35 USC §102.

Step 6 — Generate Your Prior Art Report

Your report is a PDF that bundles the invention description, search strategy, search log, and every reference you logged — formatted for attachment to a USPTO filing or handoff to a patent attorney.

✓Step 1 — Invention title + abstract. Required for any patent application. Without it, examiners can't classify your disclosure.
✓Step 1 — Key novel elements. Your claims will be built from these. At least 3 elements are strongly recommended.
✓Step 3 — Search keywords. At least 5 primary keywords ensures reasonable coverage.
✓Step 4 — Multiple databases searched. USPTO examiners expect Google Patents, USPTO, and Espacenet at minimum.
✓Step 5 — References logged. Even a "clean" search should list the top 5 closest references to show due diligence.
✓IDS duty of disclosure. Under 37 CFR §1.56, you must disclose anything material to patentability. If you have any HIGH relevance references, they must go on your Information Disclosure Statement.
Not legal advice. This report helps you do a thorough prior art search and organize results, but it is not a legal opinion on patentability. For a formal opinion or to file a patent application, work with a registered patent attorney or agent (USPTO OED roster).
